# Fax (Cloud Fax vs Traditional)

## What Is This?

Fax remains a stubbornly persistent communication method in healthcare. Despite decades of predictions about its demise, fax is still the primary mechanism for transmitting referral letters, prior authorization requests, specialist consultation notes, lab orders, medical records requests, and prescription-related communications between healthcare entities. The reason is simple: fax is considered a HIPAA-compliant transmission method, it requires no mutual technical agreement between sender and receiver, and the entire healthcare ecosystem has built workflows around it.

Traditional fax uses a dedicated phone line and a physical fax machine to send and receive documents. Cloud fax (also called internet fax, eFax, or virtual fax) eliminates the physical machine and phone line by routing faxes through an internet-based service. Outbound faxes are sent from a computer, EHR system, or mobile device. Inbound faxes arrive as PDF attachments in email, a web portal, or directly into your EHR system.

The key difference for a practice is workflow efficiency. With a traditional fax machine, someone must physically walk to the machine, retrieve printed pages, sort them by recipient or patient, scan them into the EHR (creating a second handling step), and file or shred the paper copies. With cloud fax, inbound faxes arrive digitally, can be automatically routed based on rules, and imported into the EHR without printing or scanning. Outbound faxes can be sent directly from the EHR or document management system without printing.

Cloud fax services include eFax (now part of Consensus Cloud Solutions), SRFax, mFax, RingCentral Fax, Concord Cloud Fax, and EHR-integrated fax solutions from vendors like Updox and Kno2. Pricing ranges from $15 to $50 per month for small-volume plans to several hundred dollars per month for high-volume practices, with many services charging per-page fees beyond a monthly allotment.

## Why Does a Primary Care Practice Need This?

A primary care practice is the hub of the referral network. You send referrals to specialists, receive consultation notes back. You order labs and imaging, receive results. You manage prior authorizations with payers. You coordinate with home health agencies, DME suppliers, pharmacies, and skilled nursing facilities. Almost all of these entities communicate via fax.

The volume is substantial. A typical primary care provider generates 30-50 outbound faxes per week and receives 50-100 inbound faxes per week. For a five-provider practice, that is 250-500 inbound faxes per week that must be received, reviewed, routed to the appropriate provider, and imported into the correct patient chart.

With a traditional fax machine, this volume creates a bottleneck. Paper jams, busy signals, unclear thermal paper, documents that arrive overnight and pile up, misrouted faxes sitting in the tray for hours -- these are daily frustrations that consume staff time and create patient safety risks. A referral result that sits in the fax tray for two days because it was buried under other documents is a missed follow-up opportunity.

Cloud fax solves the workflow problem. Inbound faxes arrive as digital documents that can be previewed, routed, and processed without printing. Many cloud fax platforms integrate with EHR systems, enabling direct-to-chart document import. Outbound faxes can be sent from within the EHR, eliminating the print-walk-feed-wait cycle.

For practices transitioning to VoIP phone systems, cloud fax is practically mandatory. Traditional fax machines require analog phone lines, and VoIP-to-fax transmission is notoriously unreliable due to the way fax protocols handle timing and error correction over digital networks. Rather than maintaining expensive analog lines just for fax, cloud fax eliminates the dependency entirely.

## How to Decide If You Need It

If you still have a physical fax machine connected to an analog phone line, you should migrate to cloud fax. The operational efficiency gains alone justify the cost, and if you are moving to VoIP, you will need to address fax anyway.

If you already use a cloud fax service but it is not integrated with your EHR, evaluate whether an integrated solution would reduce staff time spent on document handling. The biggest efficiency gains come from eliminating the manual steps of downloading faxes from a portal, identifying the patient, and uploading to the correct chart.

If your EHR has built-in fax capabilities (some do), evaluate whether it meets your needs before purchasing a third-party solution. Epic, athenahealth, eClinicalWorks, and several other EHRs offer native or integrated fax functionality.

The one scenario where you might retain a traditional fax machine is if you have a very low volume of faxes (fewer than 10 per week), your analog lines are inexpensive, and your staff workflow is not significantly impacted. This is increasingly rare.

## Order of Operations

1. **Quantify your fax volume.** Count inbound and outbound faxes for a typical week. Estimate pages per fax. This determines your cloud fax plan size.
2. **Map your fax workflows.** Who sends faxes? What types of documents? Who receives inbound faxes? How are they sorted and filed? Where are the bottlenecks?
3. **Check EHR integration options.** Contact your EHR vendor to determine what cloud fax integrations they support natively. Many EHRs have preferred fax partners.
4. **Evaluate cloud fax vendors.** Compare pricing (monthly fee vs. per-page charges), EHR integration capabilities, HIPAA compliance, and ease of use.
5. **Port your fax number.** Your existing fax number can be ported to a cloud fax service just like porting a voice number. This is critical -- do not lose your established fax number.
6. **Configure routing rules.** Set up rules for inbound fax routing based on cover page content, caller ID, or other criteria. Configure outbound fax templates with your practice cover page.
7. **Integrate with your EHR.** Configure the direct-to-chart import workflow if supported. Define where incoming faxes queue for review and who is responsible for processing them.
8. **Train staff.** Show staff how to send faxes from the EHR or cloud fax portal, how to process inbound faxes, and the new workflow for document handling.
9. **Decommission the fax machine.** Once cloud fax is stable and the number port is complete, cancel the analog line and remove the physical machine.

## Options by Practice Size

**Solo Practice (1 provider, 2-4 staff)**
A basic cloud fax service with 300-500 pages per month included. eFax Plus, SRFax, or mFax. EHR integration is nice to have but not essential at this volume -- a dedicated fax email inbox with manual EHR import is workable. Budget: $15-40/month.

**Small Group (2-5 providers, 5-15 staff)**
Cloud fax with EHR integration and 1,000-3,000 pages per month. Prioritize direct-to-chart import to reduce staff document handling time. Consider Updox, mFax, or your EHR's native fax solution. Budget: $40-100/month.

**Medium Group (6-15 providers, 15-40 staff)**
High-volume cloud fax with full EHR integration, automated routing, optical character recognition (OCR) for document classification, and multiple departmental fax numbers. Consider Concord Cloud Fax, Kno2, or enterprise plans from mFax or SRFax. Budget: $100-400/month.

**Large Group (15+ providers, 40+ staff)**
Enterprise cloud fax with advanced document workflow, OCR, automated patient matching, compliance archiving, and API integration with EHR and document management systems. Consider Concord Cloud Solutions (enterprise), OpenText Fax, or Kno2. Budget: $300-1,000+/month.

## Vendor Landscape

**Consensus Cloud Solutions** (formerly J2 Global/eFax) is the largest cloud fax provider globally. Their eFax Corporate and Consensus healthcare products serve thousands of medical practices and health systems. They have the most extensive API and the broadest integration ecosystem. However, their pricing can be opaque, and smaller practices sometimes find the per-page costs add up quickly.

**SRFax** is a Canadian company that has built a strong presence in healthcare cloud fax. Their HIPAA-compliant platform is straightforward, their pricing is competitive, and their API is well-documented. They are particularly popular with practices using Practice Fusion, Kareo, and similar PM systems.

**mFax** is a newer entrant that has differentiated on a modern user experience and healthcare-specific features. Their pricing model includes pages in the monthly fee rather than charging per-page overage, which makes costs more predictable. They offer integrations with athenahealth, Epic, and other EHRs.

**Updox** is not strictly a fax company -- it is a patient communication platform that includes cloud fax, secure messaging, telehealth, and patient engagement tools. For practices using athenahealth, Updox has a particularly deep integration. If you need multiple communication tools, Updox's bundled approach can be cost-effective. If you only need fax, it may be more than you need.

**Kno2** focuses on healthcare interoperability, bridging fax with Direct secure messaging (the protocol used for clinical document exchange under Meaningful Use and now MIPS). For practices moving toward electronic document exchange, Kno2 can serve as a transition platform, handling traditional fax while also supporting Direct messaging and emerging FHIR-based exchange.

For practices already using **RingCentral** for VoIP, their bundled fax capability may be sufficient for low to moderate volumes. It eliminates a separate vendor relationship and keeps billing consolidated. However, it lacks the healthcare-specific routing and integration features of dedicated healthcare fax vendors.

## Compliance & Regulatory Notes

**HIPAA and Fax:** Traditional fax has long been considered HIPAA-compliant because it transmits point-to-point over telephone lines without intermediate storage. Cloud fax introduces intermediate storage and processing, which means the cloud fax vendor is a business associate that must sign a BAA. All reputable cloud fax vendors serving healthcare will sign a BAA.

**Fax to Email Security:** Many cloud fax services deliver inbound faxes as email attachments. If the email system is not encrypted or the email account is not secured, this creates a HIPAA vulnerability. Ensure that the email system receiving faxes is HIPAA-compliant (e.g., Microsoft 365 with encryption configured, Google Workspace with appropriate settings). Alternatively, use the cloud fax vendor's secure portal or direct EHR integration instead of email delivery.

**Document Retention:** Faxes containing PHI are part of the medical record and subject to your document retention policies. Cloud fax platforms typically store faxes for a configurable period (30 days to indefinitely). Ensure your retention settings align with state medical record retention requirements (typically 7-10 years for adults, longer for pediatric patients) and that faxes imported into the EHR are the official record.

**The Fax Cover Page:** Always use a cover page with a confidentiality notice. This is not technically required by HIPAA, but it is a best practice and may be required by state law. Include the intended recipient, sender, number of pages, and a confidentiality disclaimer. Most cloud fax services offer customizable cover page templates.

**Misdirected Faxes:** Sending a fax to the wrong number is a potential HIPAA breach. Cloud fax mitigates this risk with address books and EHR-integrated directory lookups, reducing manual number entry. If a fax is misdirected, evaluate whether breach notification is required based on the content and the recipient.

**21st Century Cures Act:** The push toward interoperability under the Cures Act and ONC regulations encourages electronic clinical document exchange (via Direct messaging, FHIR APIs, and health information exchanges) as an alternative to fax. While fax is not going away soon, practices should be aware that the regulatory direction is toward electronic exchange. Cloud fax platforms that also support Direct messaging (like Kno2) position you for this transition.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Keeping analog lines "just in case."** Once your fax number is ported to cloud fax and the system is working, cancel the analog lines. Maintaining redundant lines at $30-50/month each wastes money.

2. **Not porting your fax number.** Your fax number is printed on every referral form, business card, and insurance directory listing. It takes effort to port, but losing your number is far worse.

3. **Ignoring the inbound fax processing workflow.** Cloud fax eliminates the physical fax machine but does not eliminate the need to review, route, and file incoming faxes. Define who is responsible for processing inbound faxes, how quickly they must be processed, and how they are imported into the EHR.

4. **Choosing based on price alone.** A $13/month fax plan that does not integrate with your EHR will cost more in staff time than a $50/month plan with direct-to-chart import.

5. **Not configuring routing rules.** If all inbound faxes go to one email address or one queue, the sorting burden falls on one person. Use multiple fax numbers or routing rules to direct faxes to the appropriate department (referrals, authorizations, records requests).

6. **Sending faxes by printing from the EHR and walking to a fax machine.** If you have cloud fax, send directly from the EHR or desktop. Every print-walk-fax cycle wastes 3-5 minutes of staff time.

7. **Failing to monitor fax delivery confirmations.** Cloud fax provides delivery confirmations for every outbound fax. Use them. A failed fax for a prior authorization submission can delay patient care if no one notices the failure.

8. **Not planning for volume growth.** If your practice is adding providers or services, your fax volume will increase. Choose a plan with reasonable per-page costs or included pages that accommodate growth.
