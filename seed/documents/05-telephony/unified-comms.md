# Unified Communications

## What Is This?

Unified Communications (UC) consolidates multiple communication channels -- voice calling, video conferencing, instant messaging, screen sharing, file sharing, and presence indicators -- into a single integrated platform. Instead of using separate tools for phone calls (RingCentral), video meetings (Zoom), team chat (Slack), and file sharing (email attachments), a UC platform provides all of these capabilities through one application with a unified user experience.

For a primary care practice, UC means your staff can make and receive phone calls from the same application they use to video conference with a specialist, send a secure message to the billing team, and share a document with the practice manager. Providers can see who is available (presence), escalate a chat conversation to a voice or video call with one click, and access all communication from their desktop, laptop, or smartphone.

The three dominant UC platforms in healthcare are Microsoft Teams (bundled with Microsoft 365), Zoom Workplace (formerly Zoom One), and RingCentral MVP. Each combines voice, video, messaging, and collaboration features. Teams has the advantage of deep integration with the Microsoft ecosystem most practices already use. Zoom has the advantage of ubiquitous name recognition and the simplest video experience. RingCentral has the advantage of the strongest telephony heritage and phone system capabilities.

UC is distinct from VoIP (which is just voice) and from secure clinical messaging platforms like TigerConnect (which focus on HIPAA-compliant clinical communication). UC is the broader platform that may encompass both, creating a single tool for all practice communication -- internal and external, clinical and administrative.

## Why Does a Primary Care Practice Need This?

Most primary care practices today use a fragmented set of communication tools. Phone calls go through one system (VoIP or analog), video visits happen on another platform (Zoom, Doxy.me), team messaging happens via personal text messages or email threads, and file sharing happens as email attachments. This fragmentation creates inefficiency, context switching, and compliance gaps.

UC reduces tool sprawl. Fewer platforms mean fewer logins, fewer vendor relationships, fewer BAAs to manage, and less training required for new staff. When your phone system, video conferencing, and team messaging are all in one platform, your staff spends less time switching between applications and more time on patient care.

UC also enables workflows that fragmented tools cannot. A front desk staff member chatting with a nurse about a patient question can escalate to a voice call with one click, then add the provider to the call. A provider reviewing lab results can start a video call with the patient directly from the same platform. A practice manager can see that the billing specialist is available (green presence indicator) before sending an urgent message.

For multi-site practices, UC is particularly valuable. Providers and staff at different locations communicate as seamlessly as if they were in the same building. Shared channels, presence visibility, and consistent phone system features across locations reduce the "us vs. them" dynamic that often develops between practice sites.

The telehealth dimension is significant. COVID-19 permanently established video visits as a care delivery modality, and UC platforms offer built-in video capabilities that can serve double duty for both internal meetings and patient-facing telehealth. While dedicated telehealth platforms (Doxy.me, Amwell) offer specialized clinical features, many practices find that their UC platform's video capabilities are sufficient for straightforward virtual visits.

## How to Decide If You Need It

Consider UC if:

- You are currently paying for separate voice, video, and messaging platforms and want to consolidate.
- Your practice has multiple locations and needs consistent communication tools across sites.
- You are already using Microsoft 365 and want to leverage Teams for voice calling (replacing or supplementing your VoIP system).
- Your providers conduct telehealth visits and you want a single platform for both internal communication and patient video visits.
- Your staff uses three or more different communication tools daily, creating inefficiency and compliance risks.

UC may not be necessary if:

- Your practice is small (1-3 providers), your existing VoIP and video tools work well, and consolidation would not significantly improve workflows.
- Your EHR has strong built-in communication and telehealth features that meet your needs.
- You have recently invested in separate best-of-breed tools (VoIP, video, messaging) and the switching cost outweighs the consolidation benefit.

The decision often comes down to this: are you spending too much time and money managing separate communication tools, and would a single platform meaningfully improve operations?

## Order of Operations

1. **Inventory current communication tools.** List every platform used for voice, video, messaging, and file sharing. Document the cost, number of users, and key features used for each.
2. **Identify consolidation opportunities.** Determine which tools can be replaced by a UC platform and which must remain standalone (e.g., a clinical messaging platform with EHR integration that no UC platform can replicate).
3. **Assess infrastructure requirements.** UC platforms, especially those handling voice calling, require adequate bandwidth and network configuration. A VoIP readiness assessment applies here as well.
4. **Evaluate the big three.** Get demos of Microsoft Teams Phone, Zoom Workplace, and RingCentral MVP. Evaluate based on your specific requirements, existing technology ecosystem, and HIPAA compliance needs.
5. **Plan the migration.** If replacing an existing VoIP system, this involves number porting, call flow configuration, and hardware provisioning (desk phones or headsets). If adding UC capabilities to an existing VoIP system (e.g., adding Teams messaging alongside RingCentral voice), the migration is simpler.
6. **Configure for HIPAA compliance.** Disable features that create compliance risks (e.g., guest access without controls, public channels, unencrypted recording storage). Enable required features (encryption, audit logging, DLP policies).
7. **Deploy in phases.** Start with messaging and video (lower risk), then migrate voice calling (higher risk due to phone number dependencies).
8. **Train staff.** UC platforms have more features than most staff will use. Focus training on the 20% of features that serve 80% of needs: making/receiving calls, joining video meetings, sending messages, and sharing files.

## Options by Practice Size

**Solo Practice (1-2 providers, 3-5 staff)**
Microsoft Teams with Microsoft 365 Business Basic or Standard is likely the most cost-effective UC option, especially if you already use Microsoft for email and Office apps. Add Teams Phone with a calling plan ($8-15/user/month) if you want to replace your VoIP system entirely. Alternatively, keep your existing VoIP and use Teams only for messaging and video. Budget: $6-22/user/month (often already included in M365 licensing) plus $8-15/user/month for calling plans.

**Small Group (3-5 providers, 10-20 staff)**
Microsoft Teams or Zoom Workplace are strong options. If your VoIP is RingCentral, consider RingCentral MVP, which provides a true all-in-one UC experience with strong telephony. Budget: $25-45/user/month for an all-in-one UC+voice platform.

**Medium Group (6-15 providers, 25-60 staff)**
A full UC platform with voice calling, contact center capabilities for your scheduling team, and analytics. Microsoft Teams with Teams Phone and Queues App, RingCentral MVP with RingCentral Contact Center, or Zoom Workplace with Zoom Phone and Zoom Contact Center. Budget: $30-50/user/month.

**Large Group (15+ providers, 60+ staff)**
Enterprise UC with operator console, contact center, compliance recording, analytics dashboards, and integration with EHR and practice management systems. Consider Microsoft Teams with Operator Connect, RingCentral Enterprise, or Zoom with enterprise licensing. Budget: $35-65/user/month.

## Vendor Landscape

**Microsoft Teams** has become the dominant UC platform across industries, with over 320 million monthly active users. For healthcare, Teams is compelling because most practices already have Microsoft 365 for email, Office apps, and file storage. Adding Teams Phone for voice calling creates a true UC experience without a new vendor. Microsoft has invested in healthcare-specific features including EHR integration, virtual visits, and clinical communication workflows. The complexity lies in licensing -- Microsoft 365 Business Basic, Standard, and Premium have different feature sets, and Teams Phone requires an additional license and either a Microsoft Calling Plan or a third-party Operator Connect/Direct Routing provider.

**Zoom** built its brand on video conferencing and has expanded into a full UC platform with Zoom Workplace (messaging and collaboration), Zoom Phone (voice), and Zoom Contact Center. Zoom offers a "Zoom for Healthcare" SKU that includes HIPAA compliance, a BAA, and healthcare-specific features. Zoom's strength is the simplicity of its video experience -- patients and external parties can join video calls without downloading software or creating accounts, making it well-suited for telehealth. Zoom Phone has grown rapidly and now serves millions of users, though its telephony features are still less mature than RingCentral's.

**RingCentral** comes from the telephony world and has added messaging and video to create a UC platform. For practices that prioritize phone system capabilities -- auto-attendant, call queues, call recording, IVR, analytics -- RingCentral offers the most robust feature set. Their video and messaging capabilities are solid but not class-leading. RingCentral also offers a strategic partnership with Microsoft, allowing RingCentral to power voice calling within the Teams interface, giving practices the best of both worlds.

**The Teams + RingCentral combination** deserves specific mention. RingCentral for Teams embeds RingCentral's phone system capabilities directly into the Microsoft Teams interface. Staff use Teams for messaging and collaboration while making and receiving calls through RingCentral's telephony infrastructure. This gives you Teams' collaboration strengths with RingCentral's telephony strengths, though at the cost of two vendor relationships and license fees.

## Compliance & Regulatory Notes

**HIPAA Compliance for UC Platforms:** All major UC platforms offer HIPAA-compliant configurations, but compliance is not automatic. You must use the correct licensing tier (e.g., Zoom for Healthcare, not consumer Zoom), configure security settings appropriately, and obtain a signed BAA. Common configuration requirements include:

- **Encryption:** Ensure end-to-end or in-transit encryption for all communication modes (voice, video, messaging). Teams and Zoom encrypt in transit by default; end-to-end encryption is available but may disable some features.
- **Recording Storage:** If you record calls or meetings, recordings must be stored in a HIPAA-compliant location. Cloud recording storage from the UC vendor is covered under the BAA, but recordings downloaded to local devices must be secured.
- **Guest Access:** Control who can be invited to meetings and channels. Disable anonymous access and external sharing where not needed.
- **Data Loss Prevention (DLP):** Microsoft Teams supports DLP policies that can detect and block sharing of PHI in messages and files. Configure these policies to prevent accidental PHI disclosure.
- **Audit Logging:** Enable and review audit logs for all UC platform activity. This is a HIPAA requirement for systems that handle PHI.

**Telehealth Regulations:** If using UC video for patient-facing telehealth visits, ensure compliance with state telehealth regulations, including informed consent requirements, documentation standards, and licensure requirements. The COVID-era telehealth regulatory flexibility has been partially formalized, but rules vary by state and payer.

**Recording Consent:** If your UC platform records calls or meetings, comply with state wiretapping laws. Configure recording notifications and obtain consent as required by your state's laws.

**FedRAMP:** If your practice contracts with government entities or participates in programs that require FedRAMP-authorized cloud services, verify that your UC platform meets the appropriate FedRAMP authorization level. Teams (GCC/GCC High), Zoom (for Government), and RingCentral (for Government) offer FedRAMP-authorized versions.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Trying to replace everything at once.** Migrating voice, video, and messaging to a new UC platform simultaneously is risky. Phase the migration -- start with messaging and video (lower impact if issues arise), then migrate voice calling once the platform is stable and staff are comfortable.

2. **Ignoring the licensing complexity.** Microsoft Teams licensing in particular is a maze. Business Basic, Business Standard, Business Premium, E3, E5 -- each includes different features, and Teams Phone is an add-on to all of them. Work with a Microsoft partner or your MSP to model the true cost before committing.

3. **Assuming UC replaces clinical messaging.** UC platforms like Teams are excellent for administrative communication and general collaboration, but they lack healthcare-specific clinical workflow features (role-based messaging, on-call schedule integration, critical result notification). Most practices benefit from both a UC platform (for general communication) and a clinical messaging platform like TigerConnect (for clinical workflows).

4. **Not configuring for HIPAA before deployment.** Deploying Teams or Zoom with default settings and then trying to retroactively lock down security is backwards. Configure compliance settings before staff start using the platform.

5. **Underinvesting in audio hardware.** UC platform quality depends on hardware. A $15 headset from Amazon produces echo, background noise, and frustrated patients. Invest in quality headsets (Poly Voyager, Jabra Evolve) for staff who spend significant time on calls. Budget $50-150 per headset.

6. **Overlooking the network impact.** Adding voice and video traffic to your network through a UC platform increases bandwidth requirements. If your network was sized for VoIP-only or data-only, the addition of video conferencing may cause quality issues. Reassess bandwidth and QoS settings.

7. **Not establishing channel governance.** Teams and other UC platforms make it easy to create channels, groups, and teams. Without governance, you end up with dozens of abandoned channels and no one can find anything. Define a channel structure upfront and assign someone to manage it.

8. **Forgetting about external communication.** UC is great for internal communication, but patients, referral sources, and vendors also need to reach you. Ensure your UC platform handles external calling, video visits with patients, and interoperability with external contacts who may not be on the same platform.
