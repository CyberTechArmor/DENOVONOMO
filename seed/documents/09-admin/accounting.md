# Accounting & Financial Systems

## What Is This?

Accounting and financial systems in a primary care practice manage the recording, classification, and reporting of all financial transactions -- revenue from patient services and insurance reimbursements, operating expenses, payroll, accounts payable, accounts receivable beyond clinical billing (e.g., rental income from subleased space, ancillary service revenue), capital expenditures, tax obligations, and financial reporting for practice owners, lenders, and regulatory bodies.

This is distinct from the revenue cycle management (RCM) and billing functions, which handle the clinical revenue side (claim submission, payment posting, denial management). The accounting system sits downstream of RCM, receiving summarized revenue data and integrating it with all other financial activity to produce the complete financial picture of the practice.

A modern accounting system for a primary care practice typically includes a general ledger, accounts payable (AP) management, accounts receivable (AR) beyond clinical billing, bank reconciliation, financial reporting (income statements, balance sheets, cash flow statements), budgeting and forecasting, payroll integration (or payroll processing), and tax preparation support. Many systems also include fixed asset tracking, project accounting for capital improvements, and multi-entity management for practices structured as multiple legal entities.

For most primary care practices, the accounting system is the source of truth for financial performance. While the practice management system tracks clinical revenue, and the bank shows cash balances, only the accounting system provides a complete, accrual-basis view of the practice's financial health, including deferred revenue, accrued liabilities, depreciation, and prepaid expenses.

## Why Does a Primary Care Practice Need This?

**Financial visibility drives decision-making.** Practice owners and managers need accurate, timely financial information to make decisions about hiring, equipment purchases, space expansion, payer contract negotiations, and compensation. Without a well-maintained accounting system, these decisions are made on gut feeling rather than data. A practice that does not know its cost per patient visit, its overhead rate by category, or its accounts payable aging is flying blind.

**Tax compliance is complex for medical practices.** Primary care practices operate under various legal structures (sole proprietorship, partnership, S-corp, C-corp, LLC) with different tax implications. Many practices must manage quarterly estimated tax payments, payroll tax deposits, 1099 reporting for independent contractors and locum tenens providers, retirement plan contributions (SEP-IRA, 401(k), defined benefit), and state-specific taxes. Errors in tax compliance result in penalties and interest that directly reduce partner distributions.

**Profitability analysis by provider and service line.** Understanding which providers generate the most revenue relative to their cost, which service lines (office visits, procedures, lab, vaccines) are most profitable, and how overhead allocates across the practice requires sophisticated financial tracking. This analysis informs compensation models, service line investment decisions, and strategic planning.

**Cash flow management.** Healthcare revenue is inherently delayed -- services are rendered today, claims are submitted this week, and reimbursement arrives 30-90 days later. Meanwhile, expenses (payroll, rent, supplies) are due on fixed schedules. Effective cash flow management, supported by the accounting system, prevents the crisis of insufficient cash to meet payroll despite strong revenue on paper.

**External reporting requirements.** Practices with bank loans must provide financial statements to lenders. Practices seeking acquisition or merger need auditable financials. Practices participating in value-based contracts may need to demonstrate financial capacity. Practices with investors or multiple owners need transparent financial reporting to support equitable distribution calculations.

## How to Decide If You Need It

Every practice needs accounting -- the question is the level of system sophistication:

- If you are currently managing finances through your personal bank account and a shoebox of receipts, you need an accounting system immediately.
- If you are using basic bookkeeping software (or spreadsheets) and your practice has fewer than 5 providers and straightforward financials, this may be adequate with a competent bookkeeper and CPA review.
- If you have multiple providers, multiple locations, complex compensation formulas, or revenue exceeding $2 million, you need robust accounting software and professional accounting support.
- If you are considering a merger, acquisition, or private equity transaction, you need your financials on a system that can support due diligence and potentially audit-level scrutiny.

The most critical decision is not which software to buy but whether to invest in competent accounting support -- either an in-house bookkeeper with CPA oversight or an outsourced accounting firm specializing in medical practices.

## Order of Operations

1. **Establish the legal and tax structure.** Before configuring any accounting system, confirm your practice's legal entity structure, tax elections, and fiscal year. This determines your chart of accounts, reporting requirements, and tax obligations.
2. **Design the chart of accounts.** Use a healthcare-specific chart of accounts that separates revenue by source (professional services by CPT category, ancillary revenue, rental income) and expenses by functional category (provider compensation, clinical support staff, administrative staff, occupancy, medical supplies, technology, insurance, marketing).
3. **Select accounting software.** Match to your practice size, complexity, and integration needs.
4. **Establish banking relationships.** Set up dedicated business accounts. Never commingle personal and practice finances. Consider separate operating and payroll accounts.
5. **Configure the system.** Set up the chart of accounts, vendor records, bank feeds, and recurring transactions. Establish opening balances.
6. **Integrate with other systems.** Connect or establish data flows from practice management/RCM (revenue data), payroll, banking, and credit card processing.
7. **Establish processes.** Define procedures for AP processing, expense approval, bank reconciliation, monthly close, and financial reporting.
8. **Hire or outsource accounting support.** At minimum, engage a CPA with healthcare experience for tax preparation, quarterly review, and strategic financial advice.
9. **Implement monthly close process.** Establish a routine of monthly financial close, producing income statements, balance sheets, and variance reports by the 15th of the following month.

## Options by Practice Size

**Solo Practice (1 provider, revenue under $750K):**
QuickBooks Online Simple Start or Essentials ($30-$65/month) with a part-time bookkeeper (5-10 hours/month, $200-$600/month) and annual CPA review/tax preparation ($2,000-$5,000/year). Total accounting cost: $4,000-$12,000/year.

**Small Group (2-5 providers, revenue $750K-$3M):**
QuickBooks Online Plus or Advanced ($90-$235/month) with a part-time bookkeeper (15-25 hours/month, $600-$1,500/month) or outsourced medical practice accounting ($800-$2,500/month). Annual CPA for tax preparation and advisory ($5,000-$15,000/year). Total accounting cost: $15,000-$45,000/year.

**Mid-Size Group (6-15 providers, revenue $3M-$15M):**
Sage Intacct ($500-$2,000/month) or QuickBooks Online Advanced ($235/month) with a dedicated in-house bookkeeper/controller ($50,000-$80,000/year) and CPA firm for audit/tax/advisory ($15,000-$40,000/year). Total accounting cost: $70,000-$130,000/year.

**Large Group (16+ providers, revenue $15M+):**
Sage Intacct or NetSuite ($1,000-$5,000/month) with an in-house accounting team (controller + AP/AR staff, $120,000-$250,000/year) and CPA firm for audit/tax/advisory ($30,000-$100,000/year). Total accounting cost: $180,000-$400,000/year.

## Options Analysis

**QuickBooks Online ($30-$235/month):**
The dominant small business accounting platform, used by millions of businesses including many medical practices. QBO offers four tiers: Simple Start ($30/month, 1 user), Essentials ($65/month, 3 users), Plus ($90/month, 5 users), and Advanced ($235/month, 25 users with enhanced reporting and dedicated support). Strengths: enormous ecosystem of integrations and add-ons, vast pool of bookkeepers and CPAs familiar with the platform, bank feed automation, solid reporting at Plus/Advanced tiers, affordable entry point, mobile app. Weaknesses: can become unwieldy for complex multi-entity practices, limited dimensional reporting (tracking by provider, location, and service line simultaneously requires workarounds), inventory tracking is basic, Advanced tier is the minimum for practices needing robust reporting.

**Sage Intacct ($500-$2,000/month depending on modules and users):**
A cloud-based mid-market accounting platform known for its dimensional reporting capabilities. Sage Intacct allows practices to tag transactions across multiple dimensions (provider, location, department, service line, payer) and generate reports across any combination of dimensions without maintaining separate chart of accounts segments. Strengths: best-in-class multi-dimensional reporting (ideal for practices that need to analyze profitability by provider, location, and service line), strong multi-entity management, automated revenue recognition, good API for integrations, AICPA preferred. Weaknesses: significantly higher cost than QuickBooks, steeper learning curve, implementation typically requires a consulting partner ($5,000-$20,000 for implementation), overkill for simple practice financials.

**NetSuite ($999-$4,000/month):**
Oracle's cloud ERP platform providing accounting, CRM, inventory management, and business intelligence in a unified system. Strengths: comprehensive ERP capability for large practices that want a single platform, strong multi-location and multi-entity management, sophisticated reporting and dashboards, scalable to enterprise size. Weaknesses: most expensive option, complex implementation (3-6 months, $25,000-$100,000+), requires dedicated administrative staff, overkill for most primary care practices, designed for larger organizations.

**Xero ($15-$78/month):**
A cloud-based accounting platform popular internationally and growing in the US market. Offers unlimited users on all plans, bank reconciliation, invoicing, expense claims, and project tracking. Strengths: unlimited users at all tiers (unique among major platforms), clean interface, strong bank feed reconciliation, growing US ecosystem. Weaknesses: smaller US accountant/bookkeeper ecosystem compared to QuickBooks, fewer healthcare-specific integrations, reporting less powerful than Sage Intacct, inventory management limited.

**FreshBooks ($19-$60/month):**
An invoicing and accounting platform designed for service businesses and freelancers. Strengths: excellent invoicing and time tracking, very user-friendly. Weaknesses: not well-suited for medical practice accounting -- lacks the chart of accounts flexibility, payroll integration, and reporting depth needed for even small practices. Not recommended for primary care.

**Outsourced Medical Practice Accounting ($800-$5,000/month):**
Firms like DoctorsManagement, LBMC, and Medical Group Management Association (MGMA) member firms provide outsourced accounting services tailored to medical practices. Services typically include bookkeeping, monthly financial statements, payroll processing, accounts payable, and tax preparation. Strengths: healthcare industry expertise, benchmarking against peer practices, eliminates need for in-house accounting staff, scalable. Weaknesses: less real-time financial visibility, practice leaders must still review and understand financials, cost can exceed in-house staff for larger practices.

## Vendor Landscape

The small business accounting market is mature and concentrated. QuickBooks holds approximately 75% market share among US small businesses, creating a network effect -- virtually every bookkeeper and CPA is proficient in QuickBooks, and most third-party tools offer QuickBooks integration first. This ecosystem advantage makes QuickBooks the default choice for practices that lack a compelling reason to choose otherwise.

The mid-market space is more competitive, with Sage Intacct positioning as the preferred choice for growing organizations that need more sophisticated financial reporting. Sage Intacct's 2017 acquisition by Sage Group and its continued investment in industry-specific functionality (including healthcare) have strengthened its position.

For payroll, most practices use either an integrated payroll solution within their accounting platform (QuickBooks Payroll, at $45-$125/month plus $5-$10/employee/month) or a standalone payroll processor (ADP, Paychex, Gusto). Gusto ($40-$80/month plus $6-$12/person/month) has gained significant traction with small medical practices due to its modern interface, benefits administration, and HR features.

Expense management tools like Divvy (now part of Bill.com), Ramp, and Brex offer corporate cards with automated expense categorization, receipt capture, and accounting integration. These are particularly useful for practices with multiple purchasing staff and a need for spending controls and visibility.

## Compliance & Regulatory Notes

**Tax Obligations:** Medical practices must manage federal income tax (entity-level or pass-through), self-employment tax for partners/sole proprietors, payroll taxes (FICA, FUTA, state unemployment), 1099-NEC reporting for independent contractors paid over $600, and state/local taxes. Quarterly estimated tax payments are typically required for pass-through entities.

**Stark Law Financial Considerations:** Provider compensation arrangements must be commercially reasonable, consistent with fair market value, and not tied to referral volume. The accounting system should track provider compensation in sufficient detail to demonstrate compliance with Stark Law requirements during audits.

**Practice Overhead Benchmarking:** MGMA publishes annual benchmarks for medical practice financial performance. Practices should compare their overhead rate, staffing costs, supply costs, and revenue per provider against MGMA benchmarks for their specialty and size. Significant deviations warrant investigation.

**Revenue Recognition:** Accrual-basis accounting (required for practices with inventory and recommended for all practices) requires recognizing revenue when earned (when the service is rendered), not when cash is received. The accounting system must properly record revenue at the time of service and track the receivable through collection.

**Multi-State Tax Compliance:** Practices with providers licensed and practicing in multiple states (including telehealth) may have tax filing obligations in multiple jurisdictions. This is an area of increasing complexity that requires CPA guidance.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Commingling personal and business finances.** This is the most common and damaging mistake, particularly in solo practices. It compromises liability protection, complicates tax preparation, and makes financial analysis unreliable. Maintain completely separate accounts.

2. **Not reconciling monthly.** Bank and credit card reconciliation should happen monthly without exception. Unreconciled accounts allow errors, fraud, and mispostings to persist and compound.

3. **Ignoring accounts receivable from non-clinical sources.** Clinical AR is tracked by the billing system, but other receivables (sublease rent, shared services billings, ancillary revenue) must be tracked in the accounting system. Uncollected receivables represent lost revenue.

4. **Inadequate internal controls.** The person who writes checks should not reconcile the bank account. The person who approves expenses should not process payments. Separation of duties is essential even in small practices -- embezzlement in medical practices is not rare.

5. **Not producing monthly financial statements.** If leadership is not reviewing an income statement and balance sheet monthly, problems compound undetected. Practices that review financials quarterly or annually discover issues months after they could have been addressed.

6. **Using cash-basis accounting when accrual is more appropriate.** Cash basis is simpler but can dramatically misrepresent practice financial health, particularly when insurance payments are delayed or when significant expenses are prepaid.

7. **Failing to track provider-level profitability.** Without allocating revenue and direct costs to individual providers, you cannot identify which providers are operating efficiently, where compensation adjustments may be needed, or which providers need operational support.

## Recommended Implementation Timeline

**Weeks 1-2: Foundation**
Confirm legal entity structure and tax elections. Establish or verify separate business bank accounts. Engage a healthcare-experienced CPA if not already in place. Document all revenue sources and expense categories.

**Weeks 3-4: Chart of Accounts Design**
Design a chart of accounts with appropriate detail for healthcare practice management. Include dimensions or classes for provider, location, and department tracking. Review with your CPA.

**Weeks 5-8: System Selection and Setup**
Select accounting software. Configure the chart of accounts, vendor records, customer records, and bank feeds. Set up recurring transactions for fixed expenses (rent, loan payments, subscriptions). Configure payroll integration or select a payroll provider.

**Weeks 9-10: Historical Data Migration**
Enter opening balances. Migrate current-year transactions if switching from another system. Reconcile all accounts to confirm accuracy.

**Weeks 11-12: Process Implementation**
Document procedures for accounts payable processing, expense approval, bank reconciliation, and monthly close. Train staff on the new system. Establish approval authorities and spending limits.

**Month 4: First Monthly Close**
Complete the first monthly close cycle in the new system. Produce income statement, balance sheet, and cash flow statement. Review with practice leadership. Identify and address any configuration issues.

**Months 4-6: Stabilization**
Refine reporting formats based on leadership feedback. Adjust chart of accounts detail as needed. Establish budget for the coming fiscal year. Implement variance reporting (actual vs. budget).

**Ongoing: Monthly Discipline**
Close books by the 15th of the following month. Distribute financial reports to owners/partners. Conduct quarterly financial reviews with CPA. Annual budget preparation and tax planning.
