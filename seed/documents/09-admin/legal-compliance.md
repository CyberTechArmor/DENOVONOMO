# Legal & Corporate Compliance

## What Is This?

Legal and corporate compliance for a primary care practice encompasses the framework of policies, procedures, training, monitoring, and corrective action processes that ensure the practice operates in accordance with the extensive body of healthcare laws and regulations. This goes beyond clinical compliance (HIPAA, OSHA) to include the broader legal and regulatory landscape governing how a medical practice conducts business, bills for services, maintains corporate structures, manages relationships with referral sources, and interacts with government healthcare programs.

The core regulatory framework includes the Federal Anti-Kickback Statute (AKS), the Stark Law (Physician Self-Referral Law), the False Claims Act (FCA), HIPAA (beyond just the Privacy and Security Rules -- the Breach Notification Rule and Enforcement Rule as well), the OIG Compliance Program Guidance for Individual and Small Group Physician Practices, state medical practice acts, state corporate practice of medicine doctrine (where applicable), and the 21st Century Cures Act information blocking rules.

A formal compliance program, as recommended by the OIG, consists of seven elements: written policies and procedures, designation of a compliance officer, education and training, internal monitoring and auditing, effective lines of communication (including anonymous reporting), enforcement through disciplinary guidelines, and prompt response to detected offenses. While the OIG's guidance for small physician practices acknowledges that not every practice can implement a full-scale compliance program, it strongly recommends that even the smallest practices establish a compliance framework proportionate to their size.

For de novo primary care practices, building compliance into the foundation is far more efficient than retrofitting a compliance program after operations have established potentially non-compliant patterns. A compliance violation discovered during an audit is exponentially more costly than compliance built into initial workflows.

## Why Does a Primary Care Practice Need This?

**Financial penalties are severe and increasing.** The False Claims Act imposes penalties of $13,946 to $27,894 per false claim (2024 adjusted amounts) plus treble damages. A practice that consistently upcodes E/M visits -- even unintentionally -- can face millions in FCA liability. The AKS carries criminal penalties of up to $100,000 and 10 years imprisonment per violation, plus civil monetary penalties (CMPs) of up to $100,000 per violation. Stark Law violations trigger refund obligations for all tainted claims and potential FCA liability.

**The OIG actively investigates primary care practices.** While large health systems and pharmaceutical companies attract headlines, the OIG's enforcement efforts extend to physician practices. Common investigation triggers include billing pattern outliers (practices billing higher-level E/M codes at rates significantly above peers), whistleblower complaints (often from disgruntled current or former employees), improper relationships with pharmaceutical companies or referral sources, and patterns identified through data analytics of Medicare claims.

**Qui tam (whistleblower) lawsuits are common.** The False Claims Act's qui tam provisions allow private individuals (often employees or former employees) to file lawsuits on behalf of the government and receive 15-30% of any recovery. This creates a powerful financial incentive for employees to report perceived fraud. A documented, active compliance program both reduces the likelihood of violations and provides mitigating evidence if one occurs.

**Corporate governance protects practice owners.** Proper corporate structure, documented governance procedures, and compliance with state corporate requirements protect physicians' personal assets from practice liabilities. Failure to maintain corporate formalities (regular board or partner meetings, documented resolutions, proper capitalization) can result in "piercing the corporate veil," exposing personal assets to practice debts and liabilities.

**Payer and credentialing requirements.** Many commercial payers now require evidence of a compliance program as a condition of network participation. Medicare and Medicaid increasingly emphasize provider compliance as part of enrollment and revalidation. Demonstrating an active compliance program strengthens payer relationships and reduces audit risk.

## How to Decide If You Need It

If your practice bills Medicare, Medicaid, or any government healthcare program, you need a compliance program. Period. The OIG has made this expectation clear. The question is scope and formality:

- **Every practice**, regardless of size, should have: written coding and billing policies, HIPAA policies and training, OIG/SAM exclusion screening, a documented compliance point of contact, and basic fraud and abuse training for all staff.
- **Practices with 3+ providers** should add: a formal compliance plan, designated compliance officer (can be a part-time role), annual compliance training, basic internal auditing (coding audits, billing audits), and documented policies addressing AKS and Stark Law.
- **Practices with 10+ providers** should add: a compliance committee, comprehensive audit program, anonymous reporting mechanism (hotline or online), formal investigation procedures, and regular risk assessments.
- **Practices with significant ancillary services, laboratory, imaging, or referral relationships** need enhanced compliance regardless of size, as these activities create heightened Stark Law and AKS exposure.

## Order of Operations

1. **Risk assessment.** Identify your practice's specific compliance risks based on services provided, payer mix, referral relationships, financial arrangements with other entities, and ancillary services. The OIG's annual Work Plan and compliance guidance identify current enforcement priorities.
2. **Designate a compliance officer.** This can be the practice manager, administrator, or a physician leader in smaller practices. The role requires authority to investigate concerns and access to leadership. In larger practices, consider a dedicated compliance officer or outsourced compliance services.
3. **Develop written policies.** Start with the highest-risk areas identified in your risk assessment. Core policies include: coding and billing compliance, documentation requirements, HIPAA privacy and security, anti-kickback and Stark Law compliance, conflicts of interest, OIG/SAM exclusion screening, incident reporting and investigation, and record retention.
4. **Implement training.** All staff must receive compliance training at hire and annually. Training should be role-specific: billing staff need detailed coding compliance training, clinical staff need documentation and HIPAA training, and all staff need general fraud and abuse awareness.
5. **Establish internal monitoring.** Implement regular coding audits (review a sample of charts for proper documentation and coding), billing audits (verify claims match documentation), and operational audits (confirm policies are being followed).
6. **Create reporting mechanisms.** Staff must have a way to report compliance concerns without fear of retaliation. This can be as simple as an open-door policy with the compliance officer in small practices, or an anonymous hotline/online portal in larger practices.
7. **Document everything.** Compliance activities -- training attendance, audit results, reported concerns, investigation findings, corrective actions -- must be documented and retained for at least 7 years.
8. **Review and update annually.** The regulatory landscape evolves. The compliance program must be reviewed and updated annually to address new risks, regulatory changes, and findings from monitoring activities.

## Options by Practice Size

**Solo Practice (1 provider):**
Self-directed compliance using OIG guidance documents, MGMA compliance resources, and annual CPA/healthcare attorney review. Outsourced HIPAA compliance through a HIPAA-as-a-Service provider ($100-$300/month). Annual coding audit by external auditor ($1,000-$3,000). Total compliance investment: $3,000-$8,000/year.

**Small Group (2-5 providers):**
Practice manager serves as compliance officer with support from healthcare attorney and compliance consultant. Compliance manual from a template service (First Healthcare Compliance, Compliancy Group). Basic compliance software. Annual coding audits. Total compliance investment: $8,000-$25,000/year.

**Mid-Size Group (6-15 providers):**
Designated compliance officer (partial FTE), compliance committee, compliance software platform, quarterly internal audits, annual external coding audit, anonymous reporting mechanism, regular attorney engagement. Total compliance investment: $30,000-$75,000/year.

**Large Group (16+ providers):**
Dedicated compliance officer or outsourced compliance firm, comprehensive compliance software, ongoing internal audit program, external audits, whistleblower hotline, regular board-level compliance reporting. Total compliance investment: $75,000-$200,000/year.

## Options Analysis

**First Healthcare Compliance ($200-$600/month):**
A compliance management platform designed for physician practices offering policy templates, training modules, incident tracking, risk assessments, and OIG/SAM exclusion screening. Provides pre-built compliance plans that can be customized to the practice. Strengths: purpose-built for physician practices, comprehensive template library, built-in training with tracking, affordable for small to mid-size practices. Weaknesses: templates must be customized (not plug-and-play), training content can be generic, does not replace legal counsel for complex situations.

**Compliancy Group ($200-$500/month):**
Initially focused on HIPAA compliance (their "Guard" product), Compliancy Group has expanded to broader healthcare compliance. Provides guided compliance implementation with a dedicated "Compliance Coach" who works with the practice to build and maintain their compliance program. Strengths: guided implementation reduces burden on practice staff, HIPAA focus is particularly strong, includes breach assessment and response tools, coaching model helps practices that lack compliance expertise. Weaknesses: primarily HIPAA-focused (other compliance areas less developed), coaching model requires ongoing engagement from the practice, pricing adds up over time.

**HCCS (HealthCare Compliance Solutions) (Custom pricing, typically $300-$1,000/month):**
Provides compliance education, policy development, and risk assessment services for healthcare organizations. Offers an extensive library of compliance training modules covering coding, HIPAA, fraud and abuse, and specialty-specific topics. Strengths: deep compliance training library, healthcare-specific content, supports organizations from small practices to health systems. Weaknesses: less of a comprehensive platform and more of a training/content provider, may need to be supplemented with other tools for full compliance program management.

**MedTrainer ($100-$400/month):**
A healthcare workforce compliance platform combining training, credentialing, and compliance management. Offers compliance training with tracking, policy management, incident reporting, and document management. Strengths: combines training with compliance tracking, modern interface, competitive pricing, good for practices that need a single platform for compliance training and policy management. Weaknesses: newer entrant, compliance program depth less than purpose-built compliance platforms, credentialing features may overlap with separate credentialing software.

**Outsourced Compliance Officers/Firms ($2,000-$10,000/month):**
Companies like Strategic Management Services, Compliance Concepts, and Aegis Compliance & Ethics Center provide outsourced compliance officer services, including compliance program development, risk assessments, internal auditing, investigation support, and ongoing compliance management. Strengths: expert-level compliance leadership without full-time salary, objective external perspective, experience across multiple practice types, handles complex investigations. Weaknesses: expensive, less integrated with day-to-day operations than an internal compliance officer, effectiveness depends on practice leadership's engagement.

## Vendor Landscape

The healthcare compliance technology market is fragmented, with most solutions focusing on specific aspects of compliance (HIPAA, training, exclusion screening) rather than providing a comprehensive compliance management platform. This means many practices assemble a compliance technology stack from multiple vendors.

OIG exclusion screening has become commoditized, with numerous vendors offering automated monthly screening at $1-$5 per employee per month. SAM.gov is free to search directly but does not provide the automated monitoring, documentation, and alerting that dedicated screening services offer. Vendors include Streamline Verify, Verisys, and the exclusion screening modules within compliance platforms.

Coding audit services range from automated claim-scrubbing tools (built into many billing platforms) to comprehensive external coding audits conducted by certified coding specialists (CCS, CPC). AAPC (formerly the American Academy of Professional Coders) and AHIMA provide certification and resources. External coding audits typically review 20-30 charts per provider and cost $150-$300 per chart, with annual audits recommended for all billing providers.

Healthcare legal counsel is an essential component of compliance that technology cannot replace. A healthcare attorney experienced with physician practices should review the compliance program annually, advise on complex regulatory questions (particularly Stark Law and AKS), review all arrangements with referral sources, and assist with any government investigations. Healthcare attorney rates typically range from $300-$600/hour, with annual retainer arrangements of $5,000-$25,000 available for ongoing advisory services.

## Compliance & Regulatory Notes

**Anti-Kickback Statute (42 U.S.C. 1320a-7b(b)):** Prohibits offering, paying, soliciting, or receiving anything of value to induce referrals for services covered by federal healthcare programs. Violations are criminal felonies. Safe harbors exist for legitimate arrangements (employment, personal services, space/equipment rental) but require strict adherence to specific conditions. Primary care practices must be particularly careful with pharmaceutical company interactions, lab/imaging referral relationships, and patient incentive programs.

**Stark Law (42 U.S.C. 1395nn):** Prohibits physicians from referring Medicare patients for designated health services (DHS) to entities with which the physician (or an immediate family member) has a financial relationship, unless an exception applies. DHS includes clinical laboratory, imaging, physical therapy, DME, and other services. This is a strict liability statute -- intent is irrelevant. Exceptions include in-office ancillary services, employment, personal services arrangements, and fair market value. Practices with in-house lab or imaging must ensure their arrangements meet exception requirements.

**False Claims Act (31 U.S.C. 3729-3733):** Imposes liability for knowingly submitting false claims to the government. "Knowingly" includes actual knowledge, deliberate ignorance, and reckless disregard. Systematic upcoding, unbundling, billing for services not rendered, or billing for medically unnecessary services can all trigger FCA liability. The qui tam provisions make every employee a potential whistleblower.

**OIG Compliance Program Guidance:** The OIG published compliance program guidance for individual and small group physician practices in 2000 (65 FR 59434). While not legally binding, it establishes the government's expectations and is considered the standard of care for physician practice compliance programs.

**Information Blocking (21st Century Cures Act):** As of April 2021, healthcare providers can face penalties for information blocking -- practices that unreasonably prevent access, exchange, or use of electronic health information. This includes refusing to share records with patients or other providers when requested, charging unreasonable fees for data access, and implementing technology in ways that restrict interoperability.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Treating compliance as a one-time project.** A compliance binder that sits on a shelf is not a compliance program. Compliance requires ongoing training, monitoring, auditing, and updating. The OIG evaluates whether a compliance program is "effective" -- a paper program that is not actively implemented provides no protection.

2. **Not auditing coding and billing.** Internal coding audits should review at least 10-20 charts per provider per year. External audits should be conducted annually. Practices that never audit their coding are either lucky or unaware of problems.

3. **Ignoring the Stark Law for in-office services.** Practices that perform in-office laboratory testing, offer physical therapy, or provide imaging services must ensure these arrangements meet Stark Law exceptions. The in-office ancillary services exception has specific supervision, location, and billing requirements.

4. **Failing to document fair market value.** Every financial arrangement between the practice and physicians, referral sources, or entities that send or receive referrals should be supported by a fair market value assessment. This includes compensation, lease rates, and service fees. Use independent valuation when possible.

5. **No anonymous reporting mechanism.** Employees who observe potential compliance issues need a way to report them without fear of retaliation. Without this, problems go unreported until they become government investigations.

6. **Overlooking state-specific requirements.** State medical practice acts, corporate practice of medicine doctrines, fee-splitting prohibitions, and insurance regulations vary significantly. A compliance program based solely on federal requirements may miss critical state obligations.

7. **Not screening new hires and vendors.** OIG/SAM exclusion screening should occur before hire, monthly for all existing employees and contractors, and before entering into vendor relationships. Discovering an excluded individual on your payroll months after hire compounds the liability.

## Recommended Implementation Timeline

**Month 1: Risk Assessment and Foundation**
Conduct a compliance risk assessment. Identify the practice's specific risk areas based on services, payer mix, referral relationships, and organizational structure. Designate a compliance officer. Engage healthcare legal counsel if not already in place.

**Month 2: Policy Development**
Develop written compliance policies addressing the highest-risk areas identified in the risk assessment. At minimum: coding and billing compliance, documentation standards, HIPAA, anti-kickback and Stark Law, conflicts of interest, and incident reporting. Use templates from compliance vendors or professional organizations as starting points, but customize to your practice.

**Month 3: Training Program**
Develop and deliver initial compliance training for all staff. Training should cover: general compliance expectations, the compliance reporting process, role-specific compliance requirements (coding for billers, documentation for clinicians, HIPAA for all). Document attendance and content.

**Months 4-5: Monitoring and Auditing**
Implement initial monitoring activities: first internal coding audit, OIG/SAM exclusion screening for all current employees and contractors, review of all financial arrangements for AKS/Stark compliance, and HIPAA risk assessment if not already completed.

**Month 6: Reporting and Communication**
Establish the anonymous reporting mechanism. Communicate its existence to all staff. Report compliance program status to practice leadership. Address any findings from initial monitoring activities.

**Months 7-12: Maturation**
Continue regular monitoring activities. Conduct follow-up training on areas where deficiencies were identified. Refine policies based on operational experience. Prepare for annual compliance program review.

**Ongoing: Annual Cycle**
Annual risk reassessment. Annual policy review and update. Annual training for all staff. Quarterly coding audits. Monthly exclusion screening. Regular review of OIG Work Plan and enforcement trends. Annual compliance report to practice leadership or board.
