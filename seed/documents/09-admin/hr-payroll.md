# HR / Payroll Systems

## What Is This?

HR and payroll systems for primary care practices manage the employee lifecycle --- hiring, onboarding, time tracking, payroll processing, benefits administration, tax compliance, and offboarding --- through integrated software platforms. These systems replace the patchwork of manual timesheets, paper W-4s, spreadsheet-based PTO tracking, and outsourced payroll runs that many small practices cobble together.

Modern HR/payroll platforms like ADP, Gusto, Paychex, Rippling, and Paylocity provide a unified system where employees clock in, request time off, access pay stubs, enroll in benefits, complete required training acknowledgments, and manage their tax withholdings. For practice administrators, these platforms automate payroll calculations, tax filings, direct deposits, benefits deductions, workers' compensation reporting, and compliance documentation.

The pricing model for most platforms is per-employee-per-month (PEPM), typically ranging from $6--$15 per employee per month plus a base platform fee of $35--$100/month. A 20-employee practice pays $155--$400/month for payroll and basic HR. Adding benefits administration, workers' compensation, and advanced HR features increases costs to $10--$25 PEPM. Full-service platforms like Rippling that include IT device management and app provisioning range from $8--$35 PEPM depending on modules.

Healthcare practices have specific HR requirements beyond what general small businesses face: credential verification and tracking, license expiration monitoring, OSHA training compliance, HIPAA workforce training documentation, and often complex compensation structures (base salary plus productivity bonuses, hourly plus overtime for clinical staff, per-diem arrangements).

## Why Does a Primary Care Practice Need This?

**Payroll errors are expensive and demoralizing.** Manual payroll processing for a 20-person practice with variable hours, overtime, PTO accrual, and multiple pay rates is error-prone. A single payroll error --- missed overtime calculation, incorrect tax withholding, late direct deposit --- costs the practice administrator hours to correct and damages employee trust. Automated payroll eliminates calculation errors and ensures on-time payment.

**Tax compliance is non-negotiable.** Employers must file federal payroll taxes (941 quarterly, 940 annually), state income tax withholdings, state unemployment taxes, and local taxes where applicable. Late or inaccurate filings trigger penalties: IRS late-deposit penalties range from 2% (1--5 days late) to 15% (10+ days late). Payroll platforms automate tax calculations, filings, and deposits, virtually eliminating compliance risk.

**Employee self-service reduces administrative burden.** A practice manager who fields questions about PTO balances, misplaced pay stubs, W-2 requests, and benefits enrollment every week wastes hours on tasks that a self-service portal handles automatically. Modern platforms give employees mobile access to pay stubs, W-2s, PTO balances, and benefits information 24/7.

**Benefits administration complexity.** Healthcare practice employees expect health insurance (sometimes provided through the practice's group plan), dental, vision, retirement plans (401k/SIMPLE IRA), and potentially HSA/FSA accounts. Managing enrollment, deductions, COBRA notifications, and ACA compliance manually is a compliance minefield. Integrated HR/payroll platforms streamline benefits administration.

**Credential and compliance tracking.** Unlike most small businesses, medical practices must track employee credentials: clinical licenses (RN, LPN, MA certifications), CPR/BLS certifications, DEA registrations (for providers), HIPAA training completion, OSHA bloodborne pathogen training, and malpractice insurance. While most HR/payroll platforms do not have healthcare-specific credentialing modules, some (Rippling, Paylocity) offer custom tracking fields, and the discipline of centralized HR management creates a foundation for compliance tracking.

## How to Decide If You Need It

Every practice with employees needs a payroll system. Even a solo practice with one medical assistant must process payroll, withhold taxes, and file returns. The question is sophistication level.

You should upgrade from basic payroll to a full HR/payroll platform if:

- You have more than 5 employees and PTO/benefits tracking is becoming unwieldy
- You are processing payroll manually or through a basic service that does not handle tax filings automatically
- You have had payroll tax penalties or late filing notices
- Employees frequently ask the practice manager about pay stubs, PTO balances, or benefits questions
- You are about to offer health insurance or retirement benefits and need enrollment management
- You are hiring frequently and need onboarding workflow support
- You want to track employee credentials and compliance training centrally

A basic payroll-only service may suffice if:

- You have fewer than 5 employees with simple compensation (hourly or fixed salary, no bonuses)
- You do not offer benefits (or benefits are administered entirely by the insurance broker)
- Your practice manager handles HR tasks comfortably in 2--3 hours per month

## Order of Operations

1. **Audit current HR/payroll processes.** Document how payroll is currently processed (in-house, outsourced, CPA-managed), what tax filings are handled and by whom, how time is tracked, how PTO is managed, and how benefits enrollment works. Identify pain points and compliance gaps.

2. **Define requirements.** Minimum requirements for most practices: automated payroll processing, direct deposit, tax filing and payment, PTO tracking, employee self-service portal, new hire onboarding, W-2/1099 generation, and basic reporting. Enhanced requirements: benefits administration, time clock integration, workers' comp management, ACA compliance, credential tracking, and multi-state payroll (for practices with employees in multiple states).

3. **Evaluate platforms against requirements.** Demo 2--3 platforms. Involve the practice manager and any office manager who will use the system daily.

4. **Consider integration needs.** If you use a separate time-clock system (e.g., biometric time clock for hourly staff), ensure the HR/payroll platform integrates with it. If you use a separate benefits broker, understand how enrollment data flows between systems.

5. **Plan data migration.** Moving from one payroll system to another requires careful timing. Mid-year payroll transitions require accurate year-to-date earnings, tax withholding, and benefit deduction data to be migrated. Most platforms handle migration as part of implementation, but verify.

6. **Implement at a natural transition point.** The ideal time to switch payroll providers is January 1 (clean year-to-date start), though mid-year transitions are common and manageable. Avoid transitioning in Q4 (W-2 generation complexity) unless absolutely necessary.

7. **Onboard employees.** Set up employee self-service accounts. Have employees verify personal information, direct deposit details, and tax withholdings in the new system. Complete I-9 verification through the platform's electronic I-9 module.

8. **Run parallel payroll.** For the first 1--2 pay periods, verify the new system's calculations against your previous system (or manual calculations). Confirm net pay, tax withholdings, benefit deductions, and PTO accrual are correct before fully transitioning.

## Options by Practice Size

**Solo/Very Small Practice (1--4 employees)**
Gusto Simple ($40/month + $6/employee/month) or Square Payroll ($35/month + $6/employee/month). Full-service payroll with tax filing, direct deposit, and employee self-service. Basic PTO tracking and new hire onboarding included. Budget: $60--$75/month.

**Small Practice (5--15 employees)**
Gusto Plus ($60/month + $9/employee/month) or Paychex Flex Select (custom pricing, typically $60--$100 base + $5--$8/employee). Adds benefits administration, time tracking, and enhanced HR tools. Budget: $100--$250/month.

**Medium Practice (16--50 employees)**
ADP Workforce Now, Paychex Flex Enterprise, Paylocity, or Rippling. Full HR suite: payroll, benefits, time/attendance, onboarding, compliance, and reporting. Dedicated account manager. Budget: $300--$800/month.

**Large Group (50+ employees, multi-site)**
ADP Workforce Now or Paylocity Enterprise. Multi-location payroll, complex compensation structures (provider productivity bonuses, multi-rate employees), ACA compliance reporting, workers' comp integration, and advanced analytics. Budget: $800--$2,500/month.

## Vendor Landscape

**Gusto** has become the default HR/payroll platform for small businesses with 1--25 employees. Its interface is modern and intuitive --- practice managers with no HR or payroll background can navigate it confidently. Gusto handles payroll processing, tax filing, benefits administration (health, dental, vision, 401k), workers' comp, and new hire onboarding. Their automatic tax penalty guarantee (Gusto will pay penalties and interest if they make a tax filing error) provides peace of mind. Limitations emerge above 50 employees or with complex multi-state scenarios.

**ADP** is the 800-pound gorilla of payroll processing, serving over 920,000 businesses. ADP Run targets small businesses (1--49 employees) while ADP Workforce Now serves mid-market (50--999 employees). ADP's strengths are reliability (they have been processing payroll for 70+ years), comprehensive tax compliance (they operate in all 50 states plus international), and deep benefits administration. Their weakness is pricing opacity --- ADP is notorious for complex, negotiable pricing that varies dramatically by deal. Always negotiate ADP pricing; their first quote is rarely their best.

**Paychex** is ADP's primary competitor in the small-to-mid market. Paychex Flex is a modular platform that scales from basic payroll (Paychex Flex Select) to full HR suite (Paychex Flex Enterprise). Paychex also offers PEO (Professional Employer Organization) services through Paychex PEO for practices that want to outsource HR entirely. Their pricing is generally more transparent than ADP's.

**Rippling** is a newer entrant (founded 2016) that differentiates through unified HR, IT, and finance management. Rippling can provision employee laptops, set up software accounts (EHR access, email), manage benefits, and process payroll --- all triggered from a single hire event. For tech-forward practices that want to automate onboarding comprehensively, Rippling is compelling. Pricing starts at $8/employee/month for core HR and scales significantly with additional modules.

**Paylocity** targets the mid-market (50--1,000 employees) with strong workforce management capabilities including time/attendance, scheduling, and HR analytics. Their community and social features (employee recognition, company communications) add engagement tools that larger practices value.

**Justworks** offers a PEO model where Justworks becomes the co-employer, handling payroll, benefits, compliance, and HR support. The PEO model gives small practices access to large-group benefits rates (typically 10--20% lower premiums than small group plans) and removes most HR compliance burden. At $49--$99/employee/month, it is more expensive than DIY platforms but includes benefits, compliance, and HR support.

## Compliance & Regulatory Notes

**Federal payroll tax compliance.** Employers must deposit federal income tax withholding and FICA (Social Security and Medicare) taxes semi-weekly or monthly depending on deposit schedule. Form 941 (quarterly) and Form 940 (annual FUTA) must be filed on time. All major payroll platforms handle these automatically, but the practice is ultimately responsible for ensuring filings occur.

**State and local tax compliance.** Requirements vary by state: state income tax withholding, state unemployment tax (SUTA), and local payroll taxes (some cities and counties levy additional payroll taxes). Multi-state practices (e.g., a practice with a satellite office across state lines) face additional complexity. Verify that your payroll platform supports all states where you have employees.

**ACA compliance.** Practices with 50+ full-time equivalent employees are Applicable Large Employers (ALEs) subject to ACA employer mandate requirements: offer of minimum essential coverage to full-time employees, affordability standards, and annual IRS reporting (Forms 1095-C and 1094-C). Payroll platforms with ACA modules track eligibility, generate required forms, and monitor compliance.

**Workers' compensation.** Every state (except Texas, which allows opt-out) requires workers' compensation insurance. Many payroll platforms offer pay-as-you-go workers' comp integration, where premiums are calculated and paid with each payroll run rather than estimated annually with a year-end audit. This improves cash flow accuracy and eliminates audit surprises.

**FLSA compliance.** The Fair Labor Standards Act governs overtime, minimum wage, and exempt vs. non-exempt classification. Medical assistants, front-desk staff, and billing staff are typically non-exempt and entitled to overtime (1.5x for hours over 40/week). Providers (physicians, NPs, PAs) are typically exempt. Misclassification is a costly error --- FLSA violations carry back-pay liability plus liquidated damages (double back pay). Ensure your payroll system is configured with correct exempt/non-exempt classifications.

**I-9 compliance.** Federal law requires I-9 employment eligibility verification for every new hire. Electronic I-9 completion through HR platforms is accepted by USCIS and creates a more reliable audit trail than paper forms. Retain I-9 forms for 3 years after hire date or 1 year after termination, whichever is later.

**HIPAA workforce training.** While not a payroll function per se, HR platforms that track training completion can document HIPAA workforce training --- a requirement under the HIPAA Security Rule. Some platforms integrate with LMS systems (see the LMS document) for streamlined compliance training tracking.

## Common Mistakes

**Using a CPA or bookkeeper for payroll who does not file taxes automatically.** Some CPAs process payroll checks but leave tax filings to the practice. If no one files the 941 or makes tax deposits, penalties accumulate silently until the IRS sends a notice. Use a platform that handles tax filing and depositing automatically, with a guarantee.

**Misclassifying employees as independent contractors.** Physicians covering shifts, per-diem NPs, and locum tenens providers are sometimes paid as 1099 contractors when they should be W-2 employees. The IRS uses a multi-factor test to determine classification. Misclassification triggers back taxes, penalties, and potential fraud allegations. When in doubt, consult an employment attorney.

**Not setting up PTO accrual correctly.** PTO policies that differ by tenure (e.g., 2 weeks for years 1--3, 3 weeks for years 4+) require careful configuration. Incorrect accrual rates lead to either employee complaints (too little PTO) or financial liability (accrued PTO on the books that must be paid out at termination in some states).

**Ignoring state-specific leave requirements.** Paid sick leave laws, paid family leave, and other state-mandated leave programs have proliferated. California, New York, Washington, Colorado, Oregon, and many other states have mandatory paid leave requirements. Your payroll platform must be configured to track and comply with applicable state leave laws.

**Not reviewing payroll reports before processing.** Even with automated systems, a human should review the payroll summary before processing. A data entry error (extra zero on hours, wrong pay rate) caught before direct deposit is a minor inconvenience; caught after, it is a correction that takes weeks and frustrates the employee.

**Failing to offboard properly.** When an employee leaves (voluntarily or involuntarily), a systematic offboarding process is critical: final paycheck within the timeframe required by state law (immediately to 72 hours depending on the state), COBRA notification, benefits termination, system access revocation (EHR, email, building access), and return of practice property. HR platforms with offboarding checklists prevent missed steps.

## Recommended Implementation Timeline

**Weeks 1--2:** Audit current payroll and HR processes. Document all employees, pay rates, schedules, PTO policies, benefits, and tax registrations. Identify pain points and compliance gaps.

**Weeks 3--4:** Evaluate platforms. Demo Gusto, Paychex, and one other platform appropriate for your size. Assess usability, integration with your time clock (if applicable), benefits administration capabilities, and reporting. Get pricing quotes in writing.

**Weeks 5--6:** Select platform and begin implementation. Sign contract, complete company setup (EIN, state tax registrations, bank account for direct deposit, workers' comp policy), and provide employee roster.

**Weeks 7--8:** Configure payroll. Set up pay schedules, employee profiles, pay rates, PTO accrual rules, tax withholdings, benefit deductions, and direct deposit. Migrate year-to-date payroll data if transitioning mid-year.

**Week 9:** Employee onboarding. Invite all employees to create self-service accounts. Have employees verify personal information, W-4 withholdings, direct deposit, and benefits enrollment.

**Week 10:** First payroll run. Process payroll through the new system. Compare calculations against previous system or manual calculations. Verify net pay, taxes, deductions, and deposits.

**Week 11:** Second payroll verification. Run the second payroll cycle. Address any issues from the first run. Confirm tax deposits and filings are occurring correctly.

**Week 12:** Decommission old system. Once two payroll cycles have processed correctly, discontinue the previous payroll method. Archive historical records.

**Ongoing:** Each pay period: review payroll summary before processing. Monthly: review tax deposit confirmations. Quarterly: review 941 filing. Annually: W-2 generation and distribution (January), benefits renewal and open enrollment, workers' comp audit, and payroll system configuration review.
