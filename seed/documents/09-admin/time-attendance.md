# Time & Attendance

## What Is This?

Time and attendance (T&A) systems track when employees start and stop working, manage paid time off (PTO) accruals and requests, record meal and rest breaks, and generate the data needed for accurate payroll processing. In a primary care practice context, this encompasses clinical and administrative staff including medical assistants, front desk staff, billing specialists, nurses, lab technicians, and in some cases, employed physicians and advanced practice providers (APPs).

Modern T&A systems have evolved well beyond the mechanical punch clock. Today's platforms offer mobile clock-in/out with GPS verification, biometric authentication (fingerprint or facial recognition), automated overtime calculations, labor law compliance rules engines, integration with payroll and HR systems, and real-time dashboards showing who is on the clock, who is approaching overtime, and where staffing gaps exist.

For primary care practices, T&A intersects directly with operational efficiency and compliance. Practices must comply with the Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA) at the federal level and a patchwork of state and local labor laws governing overtime, meal breaks, rest periods, predictive scheduling, and minimum wage. Manual timekeeping -- paper timesheets, honor-system reporting, or manager-entered hours -- creates significant exposure to wage and hour claims, which represent the largest category of employment litigation in the United States.

## Why Does a Primary Care Practice Need This?

**Payroll accuracy.** Labor typically represents 50-60% of a primary care practice's operating expenses. Even small timekeeping errors -- rounding discrepancies, missed overtime calculations, incorrect PTO deductions -- compound across pay periods and staff members. The American Payroll Association estimates that manual time tracking has an error rate of 1-8%, translating to thousands of dollars in annual overpayment or, worse, underpayment that creates legal liability.

**Wage and hour compliance.** The Department of Labor's Wage and Hour Division recovered over $274 million in back wages in fiscal year 2023. Primary care practices, like all employers, must accurately track hours worked, properly classify exempt vs. non-exempt employees, pay overtime for hours exceeding 40 per week (or 8 per day in states like California), and document meal and rest break compliance. A T&A system with built-in compliance rules provides a defensible record in the event of a DOL audit or employee wage claim.

**Overtime management.** Healthcare settings frequently encounter overtime situations due to patient volume fluctuations, staff call-outs, and extended clinic hours. Without real-time visibility into hours worked, managers cannot proactively manage overtime. An MA working an unplanned extra two hours on Friday might push them over 40 hours, triggering time-and-a-half pay that was not budgeted. Multiplied across staff and pay periods, unmanaged overtime can erode margins significantly.

**Operational visibility.** Knowing in real-time who is working, who is on break, and where staffing stands relative to patient schedule volume enables better operational decisions. If the afternoon schedule is heavy and two MAs called out, the practice manager needs to know immediately -- not at the end of the pay period when reviewing timesheets.

**PTO management.** Tracking vacation, sick time, personal days, and other leave categories manually is error-prone and creates disputes. Automated PTO accrual and balance tracking reduces administrative burden and gives employees self-service visibility into their balances.

## How to Decide If You Need It

If you have more than five non-exempt (hourly) employees, you need a time and attendance system. The question is whether your current solution is adequate:

- If you are using paper timesheets or manual spreadsheets, you should upgrade. The compliance risk alone justifies the investment.
- If your payroll provider offers basic time tracking but it lacks real-time visibility, overtime alerts, or break tracking, consider a dedicated T&A solution.
- If you have employees working across multiple locations, working varied shifts, or frequently adjusting schedules, you need a solution with mobile access and real-time capabilities.
- If you have experienced a wage and hour complaint, DOL inquiry, or employee dispute over hours, a robust T&A system is an urgent priority.

For practices with only salaried, exempt employees (uncommon in primary care beyond physicians), T&A needs are limited to PTO tracking, which can be handled by simpler HR tools.

## Order of Operations

1. **Audit current practices.** Document how hours are currently tracked, who approves timesheets, how overtime is calculated, and how data flows to payroll. Identify pain points, error sources, and compliance gaps.
2. **Review labor law requirements.** Confirm federal, state, and local requirements for overtime, meal/rest breaks, and recordkeeping. State requirements vary dramatically -- California, New York, and Oregon have particularly complex rules.
3. **Define requirements.** Determine: number of employees, locations, shift patterns, clock-in methods needed (kiosk, mobile, web), integration requirements (payroll system, scheduling software), PTO policies, and reporting needs.
4. **Select a system.** Evaluate vendors against requirements, budget, and ease of use.
5. **Configure policies.** Program overtime rules, break policies, PTO accrual schedules, rounding rules (if any), and approval workflows into the system.
6. **Deploy hardware (if applicable).** Install time clocks, tablets for kiosk mode, or configure mobile apps on employee devices.
7. **Train staff.** Train all employees on clock-in/out procedures and managers on approval workflows, overtime monitoring, and exception handling.
8. **Run parallel.** Operate the new system alongside the old method for 1-2 pay periods to verify accuracy.
9. **Go live and monitor.** Switch fully to the new system. Monitor closely for the first month, addressing issues promptly.

## Options by Practice Size

**Solo Practice (1 provider, fewer than 5 staff):**
Basic time tracking through your payroll provider (Gusto, ADP Run) or a simple app like Homebase (free for one location). Budget: $0-$50/month.

**Small Group (2-5 providers, 5-25 staff):**
Cloud-based T&A with mobile clock-in, PTO management, and payroll integration. When I Work, Deputy, or Homebase Plus. Budget: $50-$200/month.

**Mid-Size Group (6-15 providers, 25-75 staff):**
Full-featured T&A with scheduling integration, compliance rules engine, labor analytics, and multi-location support. Deputy, UKG Ready, or Paychex Flex. Budget: $200-$800/month.

**Large Group (16+ providers, 75+ staff):**
Enterprise workforce management with advanced scheduling, labor cost analytics, predictive staffing, and comprehensive compliance management. UKG Pro (formerly Kronos), ADP Workforce Now, or Paylocity. Budget: $800-$3,000/month.

## Options Analysis

**UKG (formerly Kronos) Ready ($3-$8/employee/month) / UKG Pro ($5-$15/employee/month):**
The dominant player in workforce management, UKG (Ultimate Kronos Group) offers two primary platforms. UKG Ready targets mid-market organizations (under 1,000 employees) with time tracking, scheduling, PTO, HR, and payroll in an integrated platform. UKG Pro is the enterprise solution for larger organizations. Strengths: deepest compliance rules engine in the market (handles all 50 states plus local jurisdictions), robust scheduling, excellent analytics, proven healthcare experience. Weaknesses: implementation complexity (8-16 weeks is typical), higher total cost with implementation fees ($2,000-$10,000), interface can feel enterprise-heavy for small practices, contracts typically require annual commitment.

**Deputy ($4.50-$6/user/month):**
An Australian-founded workforce management platform that has gained significant traction in US healthcare. Combines time tracking with scheduling, task management, and communication tools. Offers tablet-based kiosk mode with facial recognition clock-in, GPS-verified mobile clock-in, and automated break compliance tracking. Strengths: modern, intuitive interface, strong mobile experience, good scheduling integration, facial recognition prevents buddy punching, affordable. Weaknesses: less depth in complex compliance scenarios than UKG, payroll integration limited to major providers, analytics less sophisticated than enterprise alternatives.

**When I Work ($2.50-$8/user/month):**
Primarily a scheduling platform that includes time tracking capabilities. Popular with shift-based workplaces including medical offices. Offers mobile scheduling, shift swapping, team messaging, and basic time tracking with clock-in/out. Strengths: excellent scheduling features, intuitive mobile app, affordable entry point, strong team communication. Weaknesses: time tracking is secondary to scheduling, compliance features less robust, limited PTO management on lower tiers, reporting less detailed.

**Homebase (Free for one location, $20-$80/location/month for paid tiers):**
A small business-focused platform offering time tracking, scheduling, hiring, and team communication. The free tier includes basic time tracking and scheduling for one location with unlimited employees. Strengths: genuinely useful free tier, simple setup, integrated hiring tools, good for single-location practices. Weaknesses: limited scalability for multi-location practices, compliance features basic compared to UKG/Deputy, payroll integration limited.

**Payroll Provider Built-in (varies):**
ADP, Paychex, Gusto, and other payroll providers offer time tracking modules that integrate natively with their payroll processing. ADP Time & Attendance ($4-$10/employee/month above payroll fees), Gusto time tracking (included in mid/premium tiers at $6-$12/person/month plus base), Paychex Flex Time ($3-$7/employee/month). Strengths: seamless payroll integration, single vendor relationship, reduces data transfer errors. Weaknesses: T&A features are typically less sophisticated than dedicated solutions, may lack real-time visibility, scheduling integration limited.

## Vendor Landscape

The T&A and workforce management market is mature and competitive. The major trend affecting primary care practices is the convergence of time tracking, scheduling, HR, and payroll into unified platforms. Vendors increasingly compete on the breadth of their platform rather than the depth of any single feature.

For healthcare specifically, compliance with varying state break laws and overtime rules is a key differentiator. California alone has complex daily overtime (time-and-a-half after 8 hours, double time after 12), meal break (30-minute unpaid break before the 5th hour), and rest break (10 minutes per 4 hours) requirements with premium pay penalties for violations. Not all T&A systems handle these nuances equally well.

Biometric time clocks are increasingly popular in healthcare settings to prevent buddy punching (one employee clocking in for another). Fingerprint readers ($200-$500/device), facial recognition tablets ($300-$800/device), and palm scanners are common options. However, several states (Illinois, Texas, Washington, New York City) have biometric privacy laws (BIPA in Illinois being the most stringent) that require written consent and specific data handling procedures before collecting biometric data.

## Compliance & Regulatory Notes

**Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA):** Requires payment of minimum wage and overtime (1.5x regular rate) for hours over 40 in a workweek for non-exempt employees. Employers must maintain records of hours worked for at least 3 years. Most clinical support staff (MAs, front desk, billing) are non-exempt.

**Exempt vs. Non-Exempt Classification:** Physicians and some APPs may qualify as exempt under the learned professional exemption. Practice managers may qualify under the administrative exemption if they meet the salary threshold ($43,888/year as of July 2024, with potential increases) and duties test. Misclassification creates significant back-pay and penalty exposure.

**State Wage and Hour Laws:** Many states exceed FLSA requirements. California, New York, Massachusetts, Washington, and Oregon have particularly complex requirements including daily overtime, mandatory break tracking, predictive scheduling, and higher minimum wages. Your T&A system must be configurable for your state's specific rules.

**Affordable Care Act (ACA) Employer Mandate:** Applicable Large Employers (50+ full-time equivalent employees) must track hours to determine full-time status and offer affordable health coverage. T&A data feeds ACA compliance reporting (Forms 1094-C and 1095-C). Most primary care practices fall below this threshold, but growing groups should be aware.

**Biometric Privacy Laws:** Illinois BIPA requires written consent and specific data policies before collecting fingerprints or facial geometry. Violations carry statutory damages of $1,000-$5,000 per scan. Similar laws exist in Texas, Washington, and several cities. If using biometric time clocks, consult your state requirements.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Relying on employees to self-report hours accurately.** Manual timesheets completed from memory at the end of a pay period are inherently inaccurate. Real-time clock-in/out eliminates this source of error and dispute.

2. **Not tracking break compliance.** In states requiring meal and rest breaks, you must document that breaks were taken. T&A systems should prompt or require break clock-out/in and flag missed breaks for manager review.

3. **Ignoring the rounding trap.** Some practices round time to the nearest quarter-hour. While legally permissible if neutral (rounding both up and down), rounding practices that systematically favor the employer create FLSA liability. Modern T&A systems that capture exact time eliminate this risk entirely.

4. **Failing to include pre-shift and post-shift work.** If MAs are required to boot up computers, prepare exam rooms, or complete charting after their scheduled shift, this is compensable time. Your T&A system should capture it.

5. **Not configuring overtime alerts.** Managers should receive notifications when employees approach 35-36 hours so they can make informed scheduling decisions for the rest of the week. After-the-fact overtime tracking is just documentation -- real-time alerts enable management.

6. **Using T&A for scheduling or vice versa.** Time tracking and staff scheduling are related but distinct functions. Using a scheduling tool as a time tracker (or assuming schedule equals hours worked) creates inaccurate records.

7. **Not backing up data.** T&A records must be retained for at least 3 years under FLSA (and longer under some state laws). Ensure your system provides data backup and export capabilities.

## Recommended Implementation Timeline

**Weeks 1-2: Requirements and Policy Review**
Document current timekeeping methods, identify pain points, and review all applicable federal, state, and local labor laws. Confirm employee classifications (exempt/non-exempt). Document PTO policies, overtime rules, and break requirements.

**Weeks 3-4: Vendor Evaluation**
Demo 2-3 solutions appropriate to your practice size. Key evaluation criteria: ease of clock-in/out for clinical staff between patients, mobile capabilities, compliance rules for your state, payroll integration, PTO management, and total cost.

**Weeks 5-6: System Configuration**
Configure pay rules, overtime calculations, break policies, PTO accrual schedules, approval workflows, and manager permissions. Set up payroll integration. Configure time clock hardware or mobile app settings.

**Weeks 7-8: Testing and Training**
Test all scenarios: regular hours, overtime, PTO, holiday pay, split shifts, multi-location. Train managers on approvals and exception handling. Train all employees on clock-in/out procedures.

**Weeks 9-10: Parallel Run**
Run the new system alongside the old method for 1-2 pay periods. Compare outputs and resolve discrepancies. This is critical -- do not skip this step.

**Week 11+: Go Live**
Transition fully to the new system. Provide extra support during the first 2-3 pay periods. Monitor for missed punches, compliance exceptions, and integration issues. Review and adjust configurations as needed after 30 and 90 days.
