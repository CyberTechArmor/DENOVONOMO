# Point-of-Care Testing

## What Is This?

Point-of-care testing (POCT) refers to laboratory diagnostic tests performed at or near the patient -- in the exam room, nursing station, or in-office lab -- rather than sending specimens to a reference laboratory. In primary care, POCT enables rapid diagnostic results (typically 2-15 minutes) that inform clinical decisions during the patient visit rather than requiring the patient to return or the provider to follow up days later when reference lab results become available.

Common POCT in primary care includes: glucose testing, hemoglobin A1c (HbA1c), rapid strep (Group A Strep antigen), influenza A/B, COVID-19, urinalysis (dipstick and microscopy), urine pregnancy (hCG), lipid panel, INR/PT for anticoagulation management, hemoglobin/hematocrit, CRP (C-reactive protein), and basic metabolic panels. The scope of available POCT continues to expand with newer platforms offering more complex assays at the point of care.

The technology ranges from simple manual test kits (dipstick urinalysis, rapid strep swabs) requiring no instrumentation to sophisticated analyzers like the Abbott i-STAT (a handheld blood analyzer performing comprehensive metabolic panels from a few drops of blood) and the Siemens DCA Vantage (a benchtop analyzer for HbA1c and urine albumin-creatinine ratio).

A critical but often overlooked aspect of POCT is **connectivity** -- the ability for POCT results to transmit electronically to the EHR. Without connectivity, results are read from the device display and manually entered into the chart, reintroducing the same transcription errors and time costs that plague other manual data entry workflows.

## Why Does a Primary Care Practice Need This?

**Immediate clinical decision-making** is the core value proposition. A patient presenting with a sore throat can be tested for Group A Strep and, if positive, prescribed antibiotics before leaving the office. A diabetic patient's HbA1c can be reviewed and medication adjustments discussed during the same visit rather than requiring a follow-up call or visit after reference lab results arrive. A patient on warfarin can have their INR checked and dose adjusted in real time.

**Patient satisfaction and retention** improve when patients receive answers during their visit. Patients who leave the office without a diagnosis and are told "we'll call you with results in 3-5 days" are less satisfied and less likely to comply with follow-up.

**Revenue generation** is significant. POCT generates direct fee-for-service revenue through CPT codes for each test performed. A practice performing 10 HbA1c tests per day at a reimbursement of $10-15 each generates $25,000-37,500 per year from a single test type. Rapid strep, flu, urinalysis, glucose, and INR testing each add incremental revenue. For many primary care practices, the POCT lab is one of the most profitable ancillary services.

**Quality measure performance** benefits from in-office HbA1c (diabetes control measures), lipid panels (cardiovascular risk measures), and urine albumin-creatinine ratio (diabetic nephropathy screening). Having results available in real time during the visit enables gap closure and improves quality scores.

## How to Decide If You Need It

**Essential POCT for virtually all primary care practices:**
- Rapid strep (Group A Strep antigen)
- Influenza A/B rapid test
- COVID-19 rapid antigen
- Urinalysis (dipstick)
- Urine pregnancy (hCG)
- Glucose (fingerstick glucometer)

These basic tests require minimal or no instrumentation, are CLIA-waived, and cost very little to implement.

**High-value POCT to consider based on patient population:**
- HbA1c: essential for practices with significant diabetic populations
- INR/PT: essential for practices managing warfarin patients
- Lipid panel: valuable for cardiovascular risk management
- CRP: useful for antibiotic stewardship (differentiating bacterial vs. viral infections)
- Basic metabolic panel: valuable for acute care and chronic disease management

**Advanced POCT for specific use cases:**
- Abbott i-STAT: comprehensive chemistry, blood gases, cardiac markers. Best for practices with urgent care volume or complex chronic disease management.
- Siemens DCA Vantage: HbA1c and urine albumin-creatinine ratio. Purpose-built for diabetes management.
- Cholestech LDX (now Abbott): lipid panel and glucose from a fingerstick. Excellent for cardiovascular wellness screening.

## Order of Operations

1. **Determine which tests to offer** based on patient population, clinical needs, and revenue potential. Start with the essentials and add higher-value tests based on volume projections.
2. **Obtain a CLIA Certificate of Waiver.** This is required before performing any POCT. Apply through CMS (form CMS-116). Processing takes 2-4 weeks. Cost is approximately $180 for 2 years.
3. **Select analyzers and test kits** based on test menu, throughput, EHR connectivity, and consumable costs.
4. **Establish quality control (QC) procedures.** Every CLIA-waived test requires documented QC per the manufacturer's instructions. This includes running control samples at specified intervals and documenting results.
5. **Configure EHR connectivity** for analyzers that support electronic result transmission.
6. **Designate and set up the testing area.** POCT can be performed in exam rooms (simple tests) or a dedicated in-office lab space (analyzer-based tests).
7. **Train staff and document competency.** All staff performing POCT must be trained and their competency documented. Most states require annual competency reassessment.
8. **Go live** and establish ongoing QC, proficiency testing, and maintenance routines.

- Consider Abbott Afinion 2 (HbA1c, CRP, ACR, lipids on one platform, $4,000-6,000)

**Medium Group / 6-15 Providers:**
- All of the above, plus:
- Abbott i-STAT 1 or Alinity i ($5,000-12,000) for comprehensive chemistry/blood gas

- Dedicated lab space with specimen tracking and barcode scanning
- POCT middleware (Telcor QML, RALS, or Orchard Harvest) for enterprise connectivity ($5,000-15,000)
- Total startup: $25,000-60,000

## Vendor Landscape

**Abbott (formerly Alere, Cholestech):** Dominant in POCT for primary care. The i-STAT platform is the most comprehensive handheld analyzer available. Abbott also markets the Afinion 2 (formerly Alere), Cholestech LDX (lipids), BinaxNOW (rapid antigen tests), and FreeStyle glucometers. Abbott's breadth of POCT products means you can build much of your in-office lab around a single vendor relationship.

**Siemens Healthineers:** DCA Vantage for HbA1c/ACR is a workhorse in primary care. Clinitek Status+ for automated urinalysis. Strong connectivity and reliability. Less breadth than Abbott but excellent in their focus areas.

**Roche Diagnostics:** CoaguChek for INR/PT management is the standard for anticoagulation clinics. Cobas b 101 is a newer platform combining HbA1c, lipids, and CRP. Strong quality and reliability.

**QuidelOrtho (formerly Quidel):** Sofia 2 fluorescent immunoassay reader provides objective, instrument-read results for rapid strep, flu, COVID, and RSV, replacing subjective visual reads of lateral flow assays. Increasingly preferred for rapid infectious disease testing because the instrument provides a definitive positive/negative rather than a subjective visual interpretation.

**Beckman Coulter:** PocH-100i for automated CBC (complete blood count). Relevant for practices wanting in-office CBC capability.

## Compliance & Regulatory Notes

- **CLIA Certificate of Waiver** (42 CFR Part 493): Required for any POCT. Apply via CMS-116 form. Renewed every 2 years ($180). Your certificate must be posted in the testing area. You may only perform tests categorized as "waived" by the FDA under this certificate. Performing moderate or high-complexity tests requires a higher-level CLIA certificate with additional requirements.
- **State laboratory licensure:** Some states (New York, Washington, Pennsylvania, Florida, and others) have additional state lab licensure requirements beyond federal CLIA. Check your state requirements before beginning testing.
- **Quality control:** CLIA requires that you follow the manufacturer's QC instructions for every waived test. This typically means running positive and negative controls at specified intervals (daily, with each new lot of test kits, or with each new shipment). QC results must be documented and retained.
- **Proficiency testing:** While not federally required for CLIA-waived testing, some states and accrediting bodies require proficiency testing (testing unknown samples from an external provider and comparing your results). It is considered a best practice even when not required.
- **Personnel requirements:** Under CLIA waived testing, there are no specific personnel requirements (no laboratory director required). However, staff performing tests must be trained, and competency documentation is strongly recommended and required by some states.
- **Result documentation:** All POCT results must be documented in the patient's medical record, including the test performed, the date and time, the result, the person performing the test, and any QC information.
- **Billing compliance:** Bill only for tests actually performed with documented results. Ensure the ordering provider's NPI is on the claim. Use correct CPT codes and modifiers (QW modifier for waived tests).

## Common Mistakes

1. **Performing POCT without a CLIA certificate.** This is a federal violation that can result in fines and sanctions. Obtain the Certificate of Waiver before performing any testing, even "simple" tests like rapid strep or urinalysis.
2. **Not performing or documenting QC.** State surveyors and CMS auditors check QC logs. Every test type must have documented QC per the manufacturer's instructions. A missing QC log can result in citations, even for waived testing.
3. **Purchasing analyzers without evaluating consumable costs.** The analyzer purchase price is a small fraction of total cost of ownership. A $3,000 analyzer with $10-per-test consumables costs far more over its lifetime than a $5,000 analyzer with $5-per-test consumables. Calculate total cost per test including consumables, controls, and calibrators.
4. **Not connecting POCT results to the EHR.** Manual transcription of POCT results into the EHR is error-prone and time-consuming. Most modern analyzers support electronic result transmission. Budget for connectivity middleware if needed.
5. **Overstocking reagents and test kits.** POCT reagents have expiration dates, typically 6-18 months. Overstocking leads to expired reagents that must be discarded. Order based on actual usage and maintain a first-in-first-out inventory rotation.
6. **Not training all testing personnel.** Every person who performs POCT must be trained, and that training must be documented. New hires must be trained before performing tests independently. Annual competency reassessment is best practice.
7. **Ignoring reagent storage requirements.** Many POCT reagents require specific storage conditions (refrigeration, room temperature, protection from light). Improper storage degrades reagent quality and produces unreliable results.

## Recommended Implementation Timeline

**Weeks 1-4: Planning and CLIA**
- Determine test menu based on clinical needs and financial analysis
- Submit CMS-116 for CLIA Certificate of Waiver (allow 2-4 weeks for processing)
- Check state laboratory licensure requirements
- Evaluate and select analyzers; request demos

**Weeks 5-6: Procurement**
- Order analyzers, test kits, controls, and consumables
- Order connectivity middleware if needed
- Set up the testing area (countertop space, power, network connection, refrigerator for reagents if needed)

**Weeks 7-8: Installation and Configuration**
- Install analyzers and verify operation
- Configure EHR connectivity; test result transmission
- Set up QC protocols; run initial QC and document results
- Establish supply ordering and inventory management procedures

**Week 9: Training**
- Train all testing personnel on each test: specimen collection, test procedure, QC, result interpretation, troubleshooting
- Document training (signed competency checklists)
- Train providers on ordering POCT and reviewing results in the EHR
- Train billing staff on correct CPT codes and QW modifier

**Week 10: Go-Live**
- Begin patient testing
- Monitor QC results daily
- Verify EHR integration is working correctly
- Review billing for correct coding

**Ongoing:**
- Daily/per-use QC as specified by manufacturer
- Monthly consumable inventory review and reordering
- Annual competency reassessment for all testing personnel
- Annual CLIA certificate renewal (every 2 years)
- Analyzer maintenance per manufacturer schedule
- Annual cost-per-test and revenue analysis to optimize test menu
