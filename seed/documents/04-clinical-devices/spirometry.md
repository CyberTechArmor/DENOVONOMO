# Spirometry & Pulmonary Function

## What Is This?

Spirometry is the most common pulmonary function test (PFT), measuring how much air a patient can exhale and how quickly. The test produces key values including FVC (Forced Vital Capacity -- total air exhaled), FEV1 (Forced Expiratory Volume in 1 second -- air exhaled in the first second), and the FEV1/FVC ratio, which is the primary metric for diagnosing obstructive lung diseases like asthma and COPD.

In primary care, office spirometry is performed using a desktop or portable spirometer connected to a workstation. The patient breathes into a disposable mouthpiece attached to the spirometer, performing forced exhalation maneuvers under coaching from a trained staff member. Modern spirometers provide real-time flow-volume and volume-time curves on the workstation display, automated quality grading of each maneuver per ATS/ERS (American Thoracic Society/European Respiratory Society) standards, predicted value comparisons based on patient demographics, and bronchodilator response testing.

Spirometry in primary care fills a critical diagnostic gap. COPD is underdiagnosed -- studies suggest that 50-70% of COPD cases remain undiagnosed because spirometry is not performed. The GOLD (Global Initiative for Chronic Obstructive Lung Disease) guidelines and USPSTF recommendations emphasize spirometry for diagnosis and staging of COPD. Similarly, asthma diagnosis and monitoring benefit from objective spirometric measurements rather than symptom-based assessment alone.

## Why Does a Primary Care Practice Need This?

**COPD diagnosis and staging** is the primary clinical driver. A post-bronchodilator FEV1/FVC ratio below 0.70 confirms airflow obstruction, and the FEV1 percent predicted determines GOLD stage (I through IV). Without spirometry, COPD is diagnosed based on symptoms and smoking history alone, which leads to both underdiagnosis (missing early-stage disease) and misdiagnosis (attributing dyspnea to COPD when it is actually heart failure or deconditioning).

**Asthma diagnosis and monitoring** is the second major use case. Demonstrating reversible airflow obstruction (FEV1 improvement of 12% and 200 mL or more after bronchodilator) supports an asthma diagnosis. Serial spirometry tracks disease control and response to therapy.

**Pre-operative pulmonary assessment** for patients undergoing thoracic or upper abdominal surgery often includes spirometry. Having this available in-office avoids referral to a pulmonary function lab.

**Revenue generation** is meaningful. CPT 94010 (spirometry without bronchodilator) reimburses approximately $25-40, and CPT 94060 (spirometry with bronchodilator response) reimburses approximately $50-75. A practice performing 5-10 spirometry tests per week generates $6,500-39,000 annually.

**Quality measures** for COPD management (MIPS, HEDIS) often reference spirometry for diagnosis confirmation. Having in-office spirometry enables you to meet these quality benchmarks without referral.

## How to Decide If You Need It

**You need in-office spirometry if:**
- Your patient population includes significant COPD prevalence (smokers, former smokers, occupational exposure)
- You diagnose and manage asthma
- You want to perform pre-operative pulmonary assessments in-office
- You are participating in quality programs with COPD or asthma measures
- You have the staff capacity to perform and interpret the test (one trained staff member can perform 4-6 spirometry tests per hour)

**You might defer if:**
- Your practice has very low respiratory disease volume
- A pulmonary function lab is conveniently accessible to your patients
- You lack staff who can be trained and maintain competency in spirometry coaching (quality spirometry depends heavily on the technician's coaching skills)

**How many devices?** One spirometer is sufficient for most primary care practices. The test takes 15-20 minutes per patient including bronchodilator response, so a single device can support 15-20 tests per day if needed.

## Order of Operations

1. **Assess patient population and projected volume.** Estimate weekly spirometry volume based on your COPD/asthma patient panel and new diagnostic evaluations.
2. **Select a spirometer.** Prioritize ATS/ERS 2019 standard compliance, automated quality grading, EHR integration, and ease of use.
3. **Confirm EHR integration.** Verify that the spirometer can transmit results (flow-volume curves, measurements, predicted values) directly to the EHR.
4. **Designate a testing space.** Spirometry requires a quiet room where the patient can sit comfortably. The MA/nurse performing the test needs to be able to stand beside the patient for coaching. A standard exam room works fine.
5. **Train staff.** This is the most critical step. Quality spirometry depends on the technician's ability to coach the patient through proper maneuvers. The NIOSH-approved Spirometry Training Course (or equivalent) is the gold standard for training.
6. **Establish quality assurance.** Define criteria for acceptable test quality (ATS/ERS grading), implement daily calibration checks, and establish a process for provider interpretation and documentation.
7. **Configure billing.** Set up CPT codes (94010, 94060, 94070) in your billing system and ensure correct ordering workflows in the EHR.
8. **Go live** with a ramp-up period where the interpreting provider reviews every test for quality and clinical accuracy.

## Compliance & Regulatory Notes

- **ATS/ERS 2019 Standards:** The American Thoracic Society and European Respiratory Society published updated technical standards for spirometry in 2019. Your spirometer must meet these standards for accuracy, repeatability, and quality grading. All devices from ndd, NSpire/Vyaire, and MIR listed above meet ATS/ERS 2019 standards. Older devices manufactured before 2019 may not.
- **Technician training:** While there is no federal requirement for spirometry technician certification in outpatient settings, the ATS strongly recommends that technicians complete a NIOSH-approved spirometry training course. Many payers and quality programs reference this recommendation. The course is typically 2-3 days and includes hands-on practice. Available through NIOSH-approved training providers (list at cdc.gov/niosh/topics/spirometry).
- **Quality grading:** Every spirometry maneuver must be graded for quality per ATS/ERS criteria (acceptability and repeatability). Modern spirometers do this automatically, but the interpreting provider should verify quality grades before interpreting results. Unacceptable maneuvers should not be interpreted clinically.
- **Calibration/verification:** Turbine and pneumotachograph spirometers require daily calibration verification with a 3-liter syringe, documented in a log. Ultrasonic spirometers (ndd EasyOne) do not require calibration but should have verification checks per manufacturer recommendations.
- **Infection control:** Disposable mouthpieces or inline bacterial/viral filters must be used for every patient. The spirometer itself must be cleaned per manufacturer instructions between patients if any patient contact occurs.
- **Interpretation documentation:** For billing, the medical record must contain: the clinical indication, the spirometry report (flow-volume and volume-time curves, FVC, FEV1, FEV1/FVC, predicted values), quality grading, the provider's interpretation, and the clinical significance of the findings.
- **OSHA spirometry:** If your practice performs spirometry for occupational health (respiratory surveillance programs), additional OSHA-specific standards apply, including OSHA-approved technician certification.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Poor patient coaching.** Spirometry is an effort-dependent test. The quality of results depends directly on the technician's coaching ability. An untrained or poorly coached technician produces uninterpretable results that waste everyone's time. Invest in NIOSH-approved training.
2. **Skipping daily calibration on turbine-based spirometers.** If you use a turbine or pneumotachograph spirometer, daily calibration verification is required. Skipping it introduces systematic measurement errors that can lead to misdiagnosis.
3. **Not performing bronchodilator response testing.** For COPD diagnosis, post-bronchodilator spirometry is required (GOLD guidelines). For asthma diagnosis, demonstrating reversibility is key. Performing only pre-bronchodilator spirometry misses the most diagnostically important information. Perform pre/post bronchodilator testing (CPT 94060) rather than pre-only (CPT 94010) when the clinical question involves obstructive lung disease.
4. **Interpreting poor-quality maneuvers.** If the ATS/ERS quality grade indicates unacceptable or non-repeatable maneuvers, the results should not be interpreted clinically. Have the patient repeat the test or document that acceptable quality could not be achieved.
5. **Using fixed FEV1/FVC ratio of 0.70 for all patients.** The fixed 0.70 ratio for COPD diagnosis leads to underdiagnosis in younger patients and overdiagnosis in elderly patients. Using the lower limit of normal (LLN) based on age, sex, height, and race/ethnicity (available from GLI-2012 reference equations) is more accurate. Most modern spirometers calculate both.
6. **Not integrating with the EHR.** A spirometry report printed on paper and scanned into the chart loses the discrete data values needed for trending, quality reporting, and clinical decision support. Ensure electronic integration so that FEV1, FVC, and ratios are stored as discrete data elements.
7. **Buying a spirometer without investing in training.** The spirometer is the easy part. Staff training, ongoing competency maintenance, and quality assurance are what determine whether your spirometry program produces clinically useful data or noise.

## Recommended Implementation Timeline

**Weeks 1-2: Planning**
- Assess patient population and projected spirometry volume
- Select spirometer model; request vendor demonstration
- Confirm EHR integration compatibility
- Identify staff to be trained as spirometry technicians

**Weeks 3-6: Training**
- Enroll identified staff in NIOSH-approved spirometry training course (2-3 day course, may need to wait for scheduled course date)
- Complete training and obtain certificates of completion

**Weeks 7-8: Procurement and Installation**
- Order spirometer and consumables (filters, mouthpieces, calibration syringe if applicable)
- Install spirometer software on designated workstation
- Configure EHR integration
- Designate testing room and arrange furniture/equipment

**Week 9: Practice and Validation**
- Trained staff practice performing spirometry on colleagues
- Provider reviews practice results for quality and interpretation accuracy
- Test EHR integration end-to-end (order placed, test performed, results in chart)
- Establish daily calibration/verification protocol and QC log

**Week 10: Go-Live**
- Begin patient spirometry
- Provider overreads every test for the first 2-4 weeks, providing feedback to technicians on coaching quality
- Monitor EHR integration for any data transmission issues
- Verify billing is correct (CPT codes, modifiers, diagnosis codes)

**Ongoing:**
- Daily calibration/verification (turbine spirometers) or per manufacturer schedule (ultrasonic)
- Quarterly review of spirometry quality grades (what percentage of tests achieve ATS/ERS A or B quality?)
- Annual technician competency reassessment
- Annual review of spirometry volume and revenue
- Device replacement at year 7-10
