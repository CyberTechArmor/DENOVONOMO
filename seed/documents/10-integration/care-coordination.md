# Care Coordination Platforms

## What Is This?

Care coordination platforms are technology systems that support the systematic organization and management of patient care activities across multiple providers, settings, and time periods. In primary care, these platforms are most commonly associated with two specific CMS reimbursement programs -- Chronic Care Management (CCM) and Transitional Care Management (TCM) -- but they extend to a broader set of care coordination functions including Annual Wellness Visits (AWV), Remote Patient Monitoring (RPM), Principal Care Management (PCM), and Behavioral Health Integration (BHI).

Chronic Care Management (CCM), established by CMS in 2015, reimburses primary care practices for the non-face-to-face care coordination services provided to Medicare beneficiaries with two or more chronic conditions expected to last at least 12 months. CCM services include developing and revising comprehensive care plans, coordinating care between providers, medication management and reconciliation, managing care transitions, and providing patients with 24/7 access to care management services. CMS reimburses approximately $42-$142 per patient per month depending on the complexity of services (CPT codes 99490, 99439, 99487, 99489, and 99491).

Transitional Care Management (TCM), introduced in 2013, reimburses practices for care coordination following a hospital discharge, skilled nursing facility stay, or other qualifying discharge. TCM requires interactive contact with the patient or caregiver within 2 business days of discharge and a face-to-face visit within 7 or 14 days depending on medical decision-making complexity. TCM reimbursement is approximately $175-$275 per encounter (CPT codes 99495 and 99496).

A care coordination platform automates the identification of eligible patients, manages the enrollment and consent process, tracks time spent on care coordination activities, documents care plan development and updates, facilitates patient outreach and communication, and generates the billing data needed to submit CCM, TCM, and related claims. Without such a platform, the administrative burden of running these programs manually often exceeds the revenue they generate.

## Why Does a Primary Care Practice Need This?

**Direct revenue generation.** CCM and TCM represent significant revenue opportunities that most primary care practices leave on the table. A practice with 2,000 Medicare patients likely has 400-800 patients eligible for CCM (20-40% of Medicare beneficiaries have two or more chronic conditions). At an average reimbursement of $62 per patient per month (blended rate for 99490 and 99439), enrolling just 200 patients generates approximately $148,000 in annual revenue. TCM, when captured consistently for all qualifying discharges, adds another $30,000-$80,000 annually for a mid-size practice.

**Improved patient outcomes.** Care coordination is not just a billing exercise -- it delivers genuine clinical value. Regular non-visit-based check-ins catch medication issues, identify early warning signs, address social determinants of health, reinforce care plan adherence, and prevent avoidable ED visits and hospitalizations. Practices that implement robust CCM programs report 15-30% reductions in hospital admissions among enrolled patients.

**Value-based care readiness.** As reimbursement shifts from fee-for-service to value-based models, the ability to manage chronic conditions proactively, coordinate transitions effectively, and demonstrate improved population health outcomes becomes a competitive advantage. Care coordination platforms provide the infrastructure for value-based care, regardless of which specific contracts the practice enters.

**Patient satisfaction and retention.** Patients enrolled in CCM programs report higher satisfaction with their primary care practice. The regular touchpoints (monthly calls or messages) create a sense of connection and support that strengthens the patient-practice relationship and reduces the likelihood of patients seeking care elsewhere.

**Closing care gaps.** Care coordination activities naturally surface unmet care needs -- overdue screenings, lapsed medications, missed specialist follow-ups, and unaddressed preventive care. The care coordination workflow provides a structured mechanism for identifying and closing these gaps, improving quality scores.

## How to Decide If You Need It

A care coordination platform makes sense if:

- Your practice has 100+ Medicare patients with two or more chronic conditions (a reasonable minimum panel for CCM viability).
- You want to generate CCM/TCM revenue but the manual administrative burden has been a barrier.
- You are participating or planning to participate in value-based contracts (ACO, MSSP, direct primary care hybrid, or commercial shared savings).
- You have staff capacity (or can add capacity) for care coordination activities -- MAs, RNs, health coaches, or dedicated care coordinators.
- Your patient population includes significant chronic disease burden (diabetes, hypertension, heart failure, COPD, depression).

A platform may not be needed if:
- Your Medicare population is small (under 50 patients with qualifying conditions).
- You are already enrolled in a value-based arrangement that includes care management services provided by the contracting entity (e.g., some ACOs provide centralized care management).
- You prefer to outsource CCM services entirely to a third-party company (though a platform is still used -- just not by your practice directly).

## Order of Operations

1. **Assess eligibility and opportunity.** Run a report from your EHR identifying Medicare patients with two or more chronic conditions. Estimate enrollment potential (typically 20-40% of eligible patients consent to enroll). Calculate revenue potential.
2. **Decide on delivery model.** Will you deliver CCM services in-house (using your own staff), outsource to a third-party CCM company, or use a hybrid model? Each has different cost structures, control levels, and technology needs.
3. **Select a platform.** Choose technology that supports patient identification, consent management, time tracking, care plan documentation, patient outreach, and billing integration.
4. **Hire or designate staff.** CCM and care coordination activities are typically performed by RNs, LPNs, MAs, or health coaches under provider supervision. One full-time care coordinator can manage approximately 150-250 CCM patients depending on acuity.
5. **Develop care plan templates.** Create standardized care plan templates for common chronic condition combinations (diabetes + hypertension, CHF + COPD, depression + chronic pain). These templates accelerate care plan creation while ensuring comprehensiveness.
6. **Enroll patients.** Begin patient outreach and consent collection. Medicare requires documented patient consent for CCM enrollment, including acknowledgment that only one provider can bill CCM per month and that the patient may incur cost-sharing.
7. **Launch services.** Begin delivering care coordination services: monthly patient contacts, care plan reviews, medication reconciliation, care transition follow-up, and inter-provider coordination.
8. **Bill and track.** Submit CCM, TCM, RPM, and other applicable claims. Track time meticulously as CMS requires minimum time thresholds for each billing code.

## Options by Practice Size

**Solo/Small Practice (1-5 providers):**
Outsourced CCM service or entry-level platform. One part-time care coordinator can manage 50-100 CCM patients. Budget: $0 upfront if outsourcing (vendor takes a percentage of revenue, typically 40-60%), or $200-$600/month for a platform plus part-time staff costs.

**Mid-Size Group (6-15 providers):**
Dedicated care coordination platform with 1-3 full-time care coordinators. In-house delivery model provides better margins and quality control. Budget: $500-$2,000/month for the platform plus $40,000-$55,000/year per care coordinator.

**Large Group (16+ providers):**
Enterprise care coordination platform integrated with EHR, population health analytics, and reporting. Dedicated care coordination team with nursing leadership. May also serve as the infrastructure for value-based contract execution. Budget: $2,000-$8,000/month for the platform plus staffing.

## Options Analysis

**ChronicCareIQ ($200-$500/month for small practices, custom for larger):**
A physician-founded CCM/RPM platform designed specifically for primary care practices. Provides automated patient identification, consent management, care plan templates, time tracking, patient-facing app for remote interactions, and billing integration. Strengths: purpose-built for CCM/RPM in primary care, intuitive workflow, strong time tracking (essential for CCM compliance), patient engagement tools reduce care coordinator workload, designed by physicians who understand the clinical workflow. Weaknesses: focused primarily on CCM/RPM (less comprehensive for broader care coordination), smaller company than enterprise alternatives.

**ThoroughCare ($250-$1,000/month):**
A comprehensive care coordination platform supporting CCM, TCM, RPM, AWV, BHI, and PCM programs. Provides patient identification, eligibility verification, care plan management, time tracking, patient communication, and analytics. Strengths: supports multiple CMS care management programs on a single platform, strong analytics and reporting, integrates with major EHRs, compliance-focused design. Weaknesses: learning curve for the breadth of features, pricing increases with enrolled patient volume.

**Signallamp Health (Revenue-share model, typically 40-50% of CCM revenue):**
An outsourced CCM service provider that manages the entire CCM process -- patient outreach, enrollment, monthly coordination calls, care plan management, and billing -- on behalf of the practice. Signallamp provides the technology platform and the care coordination staff. Strengths: turnkey solution requiring minimal practice effort, no upfront cost, risk-free revenue model (practice earns 50-60% of CCM revenue without doing the work), experienced care coordinators. Weaknesses: practice relinquishes control over patient interactions, lower revenue per patient than in-house delivery, quality and patient experience depend on the outsourced vendor, patients may not know they are interacting with a third party.

**Prevounce ($15-$25/enrolled patient/month):**
A care management platform supporting CCM, RPM, PCM, and BHI. Provides EHR-agnostic workflows, time tracking, care plan management, and patient communication tools. Strengths: per-patient pricing aligns cost with revenue, EHR-agnostic design works with any system, strong RPM integration. Weaknesses: per-patient pricing can become expensive at scale, interface less refined than some competitors.

**EHR-Native Care Management (included in EHR or add-on module):**
Some EHRs include care management functionality. Epic's Healthy Planet, athenahealth's population health module, and eClinicalWorks' CCM module provide varying levels of care coordination support. Strengths: integrated with the clinical record, no additional vendor, familiar interface. Weaknesses: often less sophisticated than purpose-built platforms, time tracking may be basic, CCM-specific workflow support varies significantly between EHRs.

**Outsourced CCM Services (various vendors, revenue-share or per-patient-per-month models):**
Companies like Signallamp, Engooden, TimeDoc Health, and CareHarmony provide fully outsourced or hybrid CCM services. Models range from full outsourcing (the vendor handles everything) to hybrid (vendor provides technology and clinical support while the practice's staff maintains the primary patient relationship). Strengths: rapid program launch, no hiring required, vendor bears operational risk. Weaknesses: revenue share reduces per-patient margin, less control over quality and patient experience, vendor dependency.

## Vendor Landscape

The care coordination platform market has grown substantially since CMS introduced CCM billing codes in 2015. Early platforms focused narrowly on CCM time tracking and billing; today's platforms support a broader array of programs and increasingly incorporate population health analytics, social determinant of health screening, and integration with remote patient monitoring devices.

The outsourced CCM model remains popular, particularly with smaller practices that lack the staff to run the program internally. However, practices that bring CCM in-house typically achieve better margins (keeping 80-90% of revenue vs. 50-60% with outsourcing) and higher patient satisfaction (patients prefer interacting with their own practice's staff). The break-even point for in-house vs. outsourced is typically around 100-150 enrolled patients -- below this, outsourcing may be more practical.

Remote Patient Monitoring (RPM) is increasingly bundled with CCM platforms, as many CCM-eligible patients also benefit from RPM (blood pressure monitoring, glucose monitoring, weight tracking, pulse oximetry). RPM adds approximately $55-$130/patient/month in reimbursement (CPT codes 99453, 99454, 99457, 99458) and can be delivered concurrently with CCM. The combination of CCM + RPM for a single patient can generate $100-$270 per month in reimbursement.

## Compliance & Regulatory Notes

**CCM Consent Requirements:** Medicare requires documented patient consent for CCM enrollment. The consent must include: acknowledgment that only one practitioner can furnish and bill CCM services during a calendar month, that cost-sharing applies (approximately 20% of the allowed amount), that the patient can revoke consent at any time, and identification of the clinical staff who will provide CCM services. Consent can be verbal (documented in the record) or written.

**Time Documentation:** CMS requires specific minimum time thresholds for CCM billing. CPT 99490 requires at least 20 minutes of clinical staff time per calendar month. CPT 99439 covers each additional 20 minutes. CPT 99487 (complex CCM) requires 60 minutes. Time must be documented, including the activities performed, who performed them, and the duration. Time tracking is the most common area of CCM audit scrutiny.

**Care Plan Requirements:** CCM requires a comprehensive, person-centered care plan that includes: problem list, expected outcome and prognosis, measurable treatment goals, symptom management, planned interventions and identification of responsible individuals, medication management, community/social services ordered, a description of how services of agencies and specialists outside the practice will be coordinated, and schedule for periodic review and revision.

**Initiating Visit:** Medicare requires a face-to-face visit (new or established patient visit, AWV, or IPPE) to initiate CCM services. This visit must include consent collection and care plan discussion. A subsequent visit is not required monthly for ongoing CCM billing, though annual face-to-face contact is expected.

**Billing Restrictions:** Only one practitioner can bill CCM per patient per calendar month. CCM cannot be billed in the same month as certain other codes (e.g., home health care supervision). TCM and CCM cannot be billed in the same month. Verify current CMS coding guidance as these rules are updated periodically.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Underestimating the time documentation requirement.** CMS audits of CCM focus heavily on time documentation. "20 minutes of care coordination" is not satisfied by a single 5-minute phone call plus chart documentation. Activities must be logged contemporaneously with sufficient detail to support the billed time. Invest in a platform with robust time tracking.

2. **Not obtaining proper consent.** Missing or inadequate consent documentation is the most common reason for CCM claim denials on audit. Use a standardized consent script or form that covers all CMS-required elements. Document consent in the patient's record.

3. **Enrolling patients who are not engaged.** Enrolling patients who do not participate in monthly contacts creates compliance risk -- you cannot bill for services not rendered. Focus enrollment on patients who are willing and able to engage in care coordination.

4. **Ignoring the care plan requirement.** The comprehensive care plan is not optional. It must be created, reviewed, and updated. Practices that bill CCM without maintaining compliant care plans face audit risk and repayment obligations.

5. **Not calculating the financial model before launching.** CCM requires staffing investment. Calculate your break-even: a care coordinator earning $50,000/year needs to manage approximately 65-70 CCM patients (at the 99490 rate) to cover salary alone. Add technology costs, benefits, and overhead, and you need 100+ enrolled patients per coordinator for positive ROI.

6. **Failing to integrate with the EHR.** Care coordination activities should be visible in the patient's chart so that providers can see what the care coordinator is doing and incorporate that information into clinical decisions. Siloed care coordination platforms create information gaps.

7. **Not billing TCM consistently.** TCM is episodic and time-sensitive (interactive contact within 2 business days of discharge, face-to-face visit within 7-14 days). Practices that do not have a system for identifying hospital discharges promptly miss TCM opportunities. Connect to ADT alerts through your HIE.

## Recommended Implementation Timeline

**Month 1: Assessment and Planning**
Analyze Medicare patient population to identify CCM-eligible patients. Calculate revenue potential. Determine delivery model (in-house vs. outsourced vs. hybrid). Assess staffing needs. Develop financial model including technology, staffing, and overhead costs.

**Month 2: Technology Selection and Staffing**
Evaluate care coordination platforms. Demo 2-3 solutions. Select and contract with the platform vendor. Begin recruiting or designating care coordination staff.

**Month 3: Configuration and Training**
Configure the platform (care plan templates, time tracking, patient communication scripts, billing workflows). Train care coordinators on CCM/TCM clinical workflows, documentation requirements, and technology. Train billing staff on CCM/TCM coding and claim submission.

**Month 4: Pilot Launch**
Enroll an initial cohort of 25-50 patients. Focus on patients with established relationships and high engagement likelihood. Begin delivering CCM services. Bill for the first month. Monitor workflow, time tracking, and documentation quality closely.

**Months 5-6: Scale**
Expand enrollment to 100-200 patients based on pilot experience. Hire additional care coordinators if needed. Refine workflows based on pilot learnings. Begin TCM capture process (connect to hospital ADT alerts, train on TCM workflow).

**Months 7-12: Optimization**
Reach target enrollment. Analyze per-patient revenue and per-coordinator productivity. Evaluate patient outcomes (ED utilization, hospital admissions, care gap closure). Add RPM capability for appropriate patients. Report program performance to practice leadership.

**Ongoing: Continuous Growth**
Continued patient enrollment as new patients become eligible. Annual care plan reviews. Regular compliance audits of time documentation and consent. Monitor CMS guidance for program changes. Evaluate additional programs (BHI, PCM, AWV) for expansion.
