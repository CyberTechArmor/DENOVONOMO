# Patient Portal Strategy

## What Is This?

A patient portal is a secure, web-based (and increasingly mobile-based) application that gives patients direct access to their health information and the ability to interact with their healthcare providers electronically. At minimum, a patient portal provides access to clinical notes, lab results, medication lists, immunization records, and visit summaries. More robust portals add online scheduling, secure messaging, prescription refill requests, bill pay, pre-visit questionnaires, telehealth integration, and proxy access for caregivers.

Patient portals have evolved from a "nice to have" checkbox for Meaningful Use into a regulatory and competitive necessity. The 21st Century Cures Act and its information blocking provisions fundamentally changed the landscape: as of April 2021 (with enforcement ramifications ongoing), practices must provide patients with electronic access to their health information, including clinical notes (via the OpenNotes mandate), without imposing unnecessary barriers. Your patient portal is the primary mechanism for meeting this obligation.

The market is split between EHR-integrated portals (the most common model, where the portal is a module within your EHR platform) and standalone or third-party portals that aggregate data from multiple sources. Epic's MyChart has become so dominant in the health system space that patients increasingly expect a MyChart-like experience from independent practices as well.

## Why Does a Primary Care Practice Need This?

### Regulatory Compliance

The information blocking rule under the 21st Century Cures Act requires that patients have access to electronic health information (EHI) as defined by the USCDI (United States Core Data for Interoperability). This includes clinical notes, lab results, medications, allergies, immunizations, procedures, vital signs, and more. While the rule does not explicitly mandate a patient portal, a certified patient portal is the most straightforward way to demonstrate compliance. Failure to provide access can result in information blocking claims investigated by the OIG.

The OpenNotes provision specifically requires that clinical notes (office visit notes, consultation notes, discharge summaries, etc.) be made available to patients electronically without delay. This means your visit notes are visible to patients shortly after you sign them — a cultural shift that many providers initially resist but most come to accept.

### Patient Experience and Retention

Patient expectations have shifted dramatically. Consumer surveys consistently show that 70-80% of patients want digital access to their health records, the ability to message their provider, and online scheduling. Practices without a functional patient portal are at a competitive disadvantage for patient acquisition and retention, particularly among younger demographics.

### Operational Efficiency

A well-adopted portal reduces phone call volume significantly. Practices report 20-40% reductions in incoming phone calls after achieving meaningful portal adoption. Prescription refill requests, appointment scheduling, routine questions, and result inquiries can all be handled asynchronously through the portal, freeing staff for higher-value tasks.

Pre-visit digital intake (demographics updates, insurance card photos, medical history questionnaires, consent forms) through the portal reduces check-in time by 5-10 minutes per patient and improves data accuracy compared to paper forms and manual entry.

### Revenue Impact

Online bill pay through the portal increases patient collections by 10-20%. Patients are more likely to pay when they can do so conveniently through a digital interface. Automated payment plan setup, credit card on file, and statement delivery through the portal all improve the patient financial experience and your collection rates.

## How to Decide If You Need It

You need a patient portal. This is not optional. The question is which approach to take:

**Use your EHR's integrated portal if:**
- Your EHR is a major cloud platform (athenahealth, eClinicalWorks, NextGen, Epic MyChart)
- You want a single vendor to manage clinical data flow to patients
- Your EHR vendor's portal meets current regulatory requirements
- You do not need to aggregate data from multiple unrelated clinical systems

**Consider a standalone or third-party portal if:**
- Your EHR's built-in portal is inadequate or poorly designed
- You need to aggregate data from multiple EHR systems (e.g., multi-specialty group with different EHRs per department)
- You want advanced patient engagement features (sophisticated intake, campaigns, population outreach) beyond what your EHR portal offers
- You are a large organization building a unified digital front door across multiple facilities

**Consider a patient engagement platform if:**
- You want to go beyond basic portal functionality to include reputation management, patient surveys, automated recall/reminders, digital marketing, and care gap outreach
- Platforms like Phreesia, Luma Health, Relatient (Arrive Health), and Klara extend well beyond traditional portal capabilities

## Order of Operations

1. **Assess regulatory requirements** (1 week): Confirm your portal meets 21st Century Cures Act, information blocking, and USCDI requirements. Verify your EHR vendor's portal is ONC-certified for patient access.
2. **Evaluate current portal** (if applicable, 1-2 weeks): If you have an existing portal, assess adoption rates, patient satisfaction, and feature gaps. Industry benchmarks suggest 40-60% enrollment and 20-30% active use as targets.
3. **Define portal features and scope** (2 weeks): Determine which features to enable — messaging, scheduling, results release, note visibility, bill pay, intake forms, telehealth, proxy access. Each feature requires workflow decisions.
4. **Configure clinical data sharing rules** (2-3 weeks): Decide what results auto-release vs. require provider review before release. Set timing rules (e.g., lab results release after 3 days or immediately). Configure note visibility settings.
5. **Design patient enrollment workflow** (1-2 weeks): How will patients get portal accounts? In-office enrollment, email/text invitations, self-enrollment? Plan for identity verification.
6. **Configure and customize** (2-4 weeks): Branding, messaging templates, appointment type visibility, intake forms, bill pay setup.
7. **Staff training** (1 week): Train staff on portal enrollment workflow, message management, and troubleshooting common patient issues (password resets, account lockouts).
8. **Provider training on message management** (1 week): Establish expectations for message response times, triage protocols for incoming messages, and documentation of message-based care.
9. **Patient communication and launch** (2 weeks): Announce portal via email, text, in-office signage, and website. Target 50%+ enrollment in first 6 months.
10. **Ongoing adoption and optimization** (continuous): Monitor enrollment rates, active usage, message volumes, and patient satisfaction. Adjust features and workflows based on data.

## Options by Practice Size

### Small Practice (1-3 Providers)

Use your EHR vendor's integrated portal. The overhead of managing a separate portal system is not justified at this size. Focus your energy on configuration and patient adoption rather than platform selection.

- **athenahealth Patient Portal**: Solid portal with messaging, results, scheduling, bill pay. Mobile app available. Included in athena subscription.
- **eClinicalWorks healow**: Comprehensive patient app with telehealth, messaging, scheduling. Aggressive in pushing patient engagement features. Included with eCW.
- **Tebra Patient Portal**: Basic but functional. Online scheduling, messaging, intake forms. Included with Tebra subscription.
- **DrChrono Patient Portal**: Mobile-friendly, simple interface. Included with DrChrono.

Key at this size: designate one staff member as the "portal champion" responsible for enrolling patients at every visit, monitoring message queues, and troubleshooting. Without an intentional enrollment strategy, adoption will stagnate below 20%.

### Medium Practice (4-15 Providers)

Your integrated EHR portal is still likely sufficient, but you may benefit from a patient engagement platform that adds capabilities your EHR portal lacks.

- **athenahealth + Phreesia**: Phreesia adds sophisticated digital intake, patient surveys, insurance card capture, and payment processing that extend beyond athena's native portal. $400-800/provider/month for Phreesia.
- **NextGen Patient Portal + Luma Health**: Luma adds automated recall, waitlist management, broadcast messaging, and reputation management. $300-600/month base + per-provider fees.
- **Epic MyChart**: If you are on Epic (via Community Connect or direct), MyChart is the gold standard. Patients likely already have MyChart accounts from hospital encounters, reducing enrollment friction. Included with Epic.

Message management becomes a real workflow challenge at this size. Establish clear protocols: who triages incoming messages (RN, MA, provider), what is the expected response time (24-48 business hours is standard), what types of requests are appropriate for messaging vs. require a visit, and how are messages documented in the chart.

### Large Practice (15+ Providers)

At this size, you need a comprehensive digital patient experience strategy, not just a portal. Consider:

- **Epic MyChart**: If on Epic, this is your portal. Leverage MyChart's scheduling, messaging, questionnaires, proxy access, and integration with wearables/remote monitoring.
- **Custom digital front door**: Large groups are increasingly building or buying unified digital experiences that combine portal, scheduling, wayfinding, registration, bill pay, and telehealth into a single branded app. Vendors like Gozio Health, Kyruus (for provider search + scheduling), and b.well Connected Health offer platforms for this.
- **Phreesia or Relatient**: Enterprise-level patient engagement with multi-location scheduling, digital registration, payment processing, and population outreach.

At this size, budget for a patient experience coordinator or team responsible for portal strategy, adoption metrics, and continuous improvement. Portal message management may require dedicated nursing staff.

## Options Analysis

### EHR-Integrated Portal

**Examples**: athenahealth Patient Portal, eClinicalWorks healow, NextGen Patient Portal, Epic MyChart

- **Pros**: Single vendor, automatic clinical data flow, no interfaces needed, usually included in EHR subscription, ONC-certified.
- **Cons**: Feature set limited to what the EHR vendor builds, design and UX may be mediocre, limited customization.
- **Typical cost**: Included in EHR subscription ($0 incremental for basic features, some vendors charge for advanced features like telehealth or digital intake).

### Standalone Portal / Patient Engagement Platform

**Examples**: Phreesia, Luma Health, Relatient (Arrive Health), Klara, Mend, Solutionreach

- **Pros**: Best-of-breed features, often superior UX, advanced engagement capabilities, vendor-agnostic (can work with multiple EHRs).
- **Cons**: Requires integration with EHR (HL7 or API), additional cost, another vendor relationship, potential data synchronization issues.
- **Typical cost**: $300-1,000/provider/month or $500-3,000/month flat fee depending on vendor and features.

### Custom-Built Portal

- **Pros**: Complete control over UX and features, can integrate with any backend system.
- **Cons**: Extremely expensive ($100,000-500,000+ development), requires ongoing maintenance, ONC certification is complex and costly, not recommended for any but the largest health systems.
- **Recommendation**: Do not build a custom portal unless you are a large health system with a dedicated digital team.

### Aggregation Portals

**Examples**: CommonHealth, Apple Health Records, Microsoft Azure Health Data Services

- **Pros**: Patient-controlled, aggregates from multiple providers, FHIR-based.
- **Cons**: Not a replacement for your practice's portal — these are patient-side tools. You still need to expose FHIR APIs for these tools to consume.

## Compliance & Regulatory Notes

- **21st Century Cures Act / Information Blocking**: You must provide patients access to their EHI as defined by USCDI. This includes visit notes, lab results, imaging reports, medications, allergies, immunizations, clinical documents, and more. You cannot charge for portal access or impose unreasonable barriers (e.g., requiring in-person identity verification for every login).
- **OpenNotes**: Clinical notes must be available to patients electronically without unreasonable delay. Most practices release notes when signed; some implement a brief delay (24-72 hours) for sensitive results, which is permitted under certain information blocking exceptions.
- **HIPAA and patient portal security**: Portals must use encryption in transit (TLS) and at rest, strong authentication (multi-factor recommended), automatic session timeout, and audit logging. Patient identity verification during enrollment is critical — sending portal invitations to wrong email addresses is a HIPAA breach.
- **Proxy access**: You must support authorized representative access (parents for minors, caregivers for elderly/disabled patients). Proxy access for adolescents is complex due to state-specific minor consent laws. Many EHR portals handle this poorly — review your vendor's proxy capabilities carefully.
- **FHIR API requirements**: ONC's HTI-1 rule requires that certified EHRs support FHIR R4 APIs for patient access. Your portal vendor must comply with these API requirements, and you cannot block third-party apps from accessing patient data through these APIs (subject to reasonable security requirements).
- **State-specific requirements**: Some states have additional patient access requirements (e.g., California's CMIA, state-specific telehealth consent laws). Review with your compliance advisor.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Passive enrollment approach**: Simply handing patients a flyer about the portal results in <20% enrollment. Active enrollment at every visit — with staff walking patients through initial setup before they leave the office — drives 50%+ enrollment.
2. **Releasing all results without clinical review**: While the Cures Act requires timely access, most practices benefit from a brief review period for abnormal results so that providers can contextualize results before patients see them. A 3-day auto-release with immediate release for normal results is a reasonable starting policy.
3. **No message management workflow**: Unmanaged portal messages overwhelm providers, leading to long response times and burnout. Implement triage protocols: RN/MA handles refill requests, scheduling, and routine questions; only clinical questions route to the provider.
4. **Ignoring the mobile experience**: 60-70% of portal access is via mobile devices. If your portal does not have a functional mobile app or responsive mobile web experience, adoption will suffer.
5. **Not setting patient expectations**: Clearly communicate that the portal is not for emergencies, that message response time is 1-2 business days, and what types of requests are appropriate for messaging vs. a visit.
6. **Failing to document portal messages as clinical encounters**: Messages that involve clinical decision-making should be documented in the chart. Many EHRs auto-document messages, but review your workflow to ensure nothing falls through the cracks. Additionally, evaluate whether some messages qualify for billing (e-visit codes 99421-99423 or chronic care management time).
7. **Underestimating the inbox burden**: As portal adoption grows, so does message volume. Budget 30-60 minutes per provider per day for message management once adoption exceeds 30%. Some practices hire dedicated portal nurses to manage message triage.
8. **Not leveraging the portal for operational efficiency**: If you have a portal but still rely on phone calls for scheduling, refills, and routine questions, you are paying for the portal without getting the efficiency gains. Actively redirect phone callers to the portal for appropriate requests.

## Recommended Implementation Timeline

### New Portal Deployment (with EHR Implementation)

| Phase | Duration | Key Activities |
|-------|----------|---------------|
| Portal configuration | 2-3 weeks (parallel with EHR build) | Feature enablement, branding, data sharing rules |
| Intake form design | 1-2 weeks | Digital demographics, history, consent forms |
| Message workflow design | 1 week | Triage protocols, response time standards, staffing |
| Staff training | 1 week | Enrollment workflow, message management |
| Soft launch | 2 weeks | Enroll early adopter patients, test workflows |
| Full launch | Ongoing | Active enrollment at every visit, marketing |
| Adoption milestones | 6-12 months | Target 40% enrolled at 6 months, 60% at 12 months |

### Adding Standalone Patient Engagement Platform

| Phase | Duration | Key Activities |
|-------|----------|---------------|
| Vendor selection | 3-4 weeks | Demos, references, EHR integration verification |
| Integration/interface build | 3-6 weeks | API or HL7 integration with EHR |
| Configuration | 2-3 weeks | Intake forms, scheduling rules, payment setup |
| Staff training | 1-2 weeks | New workflows, dual-system management |
| Pilot | 2-4 weeks | Single location or provider group pilot |
| Full rollout | 2-4 weeks | Expand to all locations and providers |

**Key metric to track**: Portal adoption is a vanity metric if patients enroll but never return. Track active usage (at least one login per quarter) as your primary KPI. Industry leaders achieve 30-40% quarterly active usage rates. Also track message volume per provider per day — if it exceeds 20-25 messages/day per provider, you need to adjust your triage workflow or staffing model.
