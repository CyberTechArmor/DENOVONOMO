# Practice Management & Billing Systems

## What Is This?

A Practice Management (PM) system handles the business operations of a medical practice: patient registration and demographics, insurance verification, appointment scheduling, charge capture, claims submission, payment posting, denial management, accounts receivable follow-up, and financial reporting. If the EHR is the clinical brain of the practice, the PM system is the financial engine.

In modern ambulatory practice, the PM system is increasingly integrated with or embedded within the EHR platform. The standalone PM system — once the norm — is becoming less common, though it still has a role in practices with complex billing needs or those using billing services that prefer their own platforms. The line between EHR and PM has blurred to the point where many vendors market a single "EHR/PM" or "practice management suite" that covers both.

Closely related to the PM system is the clearinghouse, which acts as the intermediary between your practice and payers for electronic claims submission, eligibility verification, and remittance advice. Some PM systems include a built-in clearinghouse; others require a separate subscription.

Revenue Cycle Management (RCM) services go a step further, providing outsourced or semi-outsourced billing and collections support on top of the PM technology. This is a critical distinction: the PM system is the tool, while RCM is the service layer that operates the tool.

## Why Does a Primary Care Practice Need This?

Revenue is the lifeblood of any practice, and the PM system is how you ensure you get paid accurately and promptly for the care you deliver. Consider the financial stakes:

- The average primary care practice loses 5-10% of potential revenue to billing errors, missed charges, coding mistakes, and untimely follow-up on denials.
- Days in accounts receivable (A/R) directly impacts cash flow. A well-run PM system targets <30 days for commercial payers and <45 days overall. Poorly managed A/R can exceed 60-90 days and threaten practice viability.
- Claim denial rates average 5-10% across the industry. Each denied claim costs $25-50 in rework time. A good PM system with integrated scrubbing catches errors before submission.
- Eligibility verification failures account for a significant portion of denials. Real-time eligibility checking integrated into the scheduling and check-in workflow prevents seeing uninsured or out-of-network patients unknowingly.

Beyond revenue, the PM system drives operational efficiency. Scheduling optimization, provider productivity reporting, payer mix analysis, and financial dashboards all depend on PM data. If you are participating in value-based contracts (ACO, capitation, shared savings), the PM system tracks attributed patients, quality-linked payments, and care management fees.

## How to Decide If You Need It

Every practice needs practice management functionality. The real question is whether you need a standalone PM system or whether the PM module integrated with your EHR is sufficient.

**Use integrated EHR/PM when:**
- You are a small-to-medium practice (1-15 providers) with straightforward billing
- You want to minimize interfaces and data reconciliation
- Your EHR vendor offers solid PM/billing functionality (athenahealth, eClinicalWorks, NextGen all offer strong integrated PM)
- You want a single vendor relationship and support channel

**Consider standalone PM when:**
- You use an outsourced billing company that has standardized on a specific PM platform
- You have complex multi-entity billing (multiple TINs, facility/professional fee splits)
- Your EHR's built-in PM module is weak (some specialty EHRs have bolted-on, mediocre PM)
- You are a large group with dedicated billing department that needs advanced RCM tools

**Consider RCM services when:**
- You do not have experienced billing staff and cannot recruit them (common in smaller markets)
- Your denial rate exceeds 8-10% and your A/R days exceed 45
- You want to focus clinical leadership time on care delivery rather than billing operations
- You are a startup practice and need to maximize collections from day one

## Order of Operations

1. **Assess current billing performance** (2 weeks): If switching or starting, benchmark current denial rates, A/R days, collection ratios, and cost to collect. This establishes your baseline.
2. **Decide integrated vs. standalone** (1-2 weeks): This decision should happen concurrently with or immediately after EHR selection.
3. **Evaluate RCM service needs** (2 weeks): Determine if you will bill in-house, outsource fully, or use a hybrid model. Get proposals from RCM services if applicable.
4. **Select clearinghouse** (1-2 weeks): If not bundled with your PM or EHR, select a clearinghouse. Most practices use whichever clearinghouse their PM vendor partners with.
5. **Configure fee schedules and payer contracts** (2-4 weeks): Load your contracted rates, build fee schedules, set up payer-specific billing rules, and configure authorization requirements.
6. **Set up electronic payer connections** (2-6 weeks): Enroll with payers for electronic claims, ERA (Electronic Remittance Advice), and real-time eligibility. This step has external dependencies and often causes delays.
7. **Configure workflows** (2-3 weeks): Build scheduling templates, check-in workflows, charge capture workflows, and denial management queues.
8. **Train staff** (1-2 weeks): Front desk (scheduling, registration, eligibility), clinical staff (charge capture, order entry), billing staff (claims, posting, denials, reporting).
9. **Go-live and parallel processing** (2-4 weeks): If migrating, run parallel billing in old and new systems to verify accuracy. Monitor first few claim batches closely.
10. **Ongoing optimization** (continuous): Monthly review of KPIs, denial pattern analysis, fee schedule updates, and workflow refinement.

## Options by Practice Size

### Small Practice (1-3 Providers)

At this size, integrated EHR/PM is almost always the right choice. You likely have 1-2 front desk staff handling scheduling and basic billing, and possibly a part-time or outsourced biller.

- **athenahealth**: Best-in-class RCM services bundled with PM. Their rules engine scrubs claims before submission and their team works denials on your behalf. Pricing is typically a percentage of collections (4-8%), which aligns incentives but can be expensive for high-revenue practices.
- **Tebra (Kareo)**: Affordable PM with solid small-practice features. $150-300/provider/month for PM. Good clearinghouse integration. RCM services available but less sophisticated than athena.
- **AdvancedMD**: Strong standalone PM that also offers EHR. Popular with billing services. $300-500/provider/month for PM+EHR bundle.
- **CollaborateMD**: Affordable, cloud-based PM ($230-350/month per provider). Good for practices that want clean, simple billing software without a lot of complexity.

For practices this size, outsourcing billing to an RCM service or billing company is often the most cost-effective approach. In-house billing requires at least one experienced medical biller ($45,000-65,000/year salary), which is a significant overhead for a 1-3 provider practice.

### Medium Practice (4-15 Providers)

You likely have a dedicated billing team of 2-5 people. The PM system needs to support multiple providers, potentially multiple locations, more sophisticated reporting, and higher claim volumes.

- **athenahealth**: Continues to excel at this size. The RCM services scale with you, and the network intelligence (benchmarking against similar practices) provides actionable insights.
- **NextGen PM**: Strong mid-market PM with good reporting and multi-location support. Integrates tightly with NextGen EHR. $250-450/provider/month.
- **eClinicalWorks PM**: Integrated with their EHR. Adequate for most practices but less sophisticated RCM support than athena.
- **AdvancedMD**: Popular choice for groups that want a powerful PM with billing service options. Their analytics and reporting are strong.
- **Waystar (formerly ZirMed + Navicure)**: Not a PM per se, but the leading clearinghouse/RCM platform that integrates with many EHR/PM systems. Excellent denial management and analytics tools.

At this size, evaluate whether a hybrid model works: in-house billers handling day-to-day operations with an RCM service handling complex denials, appeals, and A/R follow-up.

### Large Practice (15+ Providers)

You need enterprise-grade financial management: multi-entity billing, complex provider compensation modeling, advanced analytics, and integration with accounting systems.

- **Epic (Resolute)**: If you've chosen Epic for EHR, Resolute is the integrated PM/billing module. Extremely capable but complex. Requires dedicated billing analysts.
- **athenahealth**: Can serve large groups, though some find the percentage-of-collections pricing model expensive at scale. Negotiate flat per-provider pricing above 15-20 providers.
- **NextGen Enterprise PM**: Designed for larger groups with complex organizational structures.
- **Oracle Health (Cerner) Revenue Cycle**: For hospital-affiliated groups on Cerner EHR.
- **Centricity Practice Solution**: Being sunset by GE/GEHC — avoid for new implementations.

At this size, you should also invest in dedicated revenue cycle analytics (Tableau/Power BI dashboards, or specialized tools like Phreesia Analytics or Waystar analytics) to track KPIs at the provider, location, payer, and procedure level.

## Options Analysis

### Integrated EHR/PM (Single Vendor)

**Examples**: athenahealth, eClinicalWorks, NextGen, Epic

- **Pros**: Single data source, no interface maintenance, unified workflow, single vendor relationship, usually lower total cost.
- **Cons**: Locked into one vendor's approach to both clinical and financial, PM module may be weaker than best-of-breed standalone.
- **Best for**: 80%+ of practices, especially those without dedicated billing IT staff.
- **Typical cost**: Bundled into EHR pricing ($250-800/provider/month depending on vendor and services).

### Standalone PM + Separate EHR

**Examples**: AdvancedMD PM + separate EHR, CollaborateMD + separate EHR

- **Pros**: Can choose best-of-breed for each function, billing company can use their preferred platform.
- **Cons**: Requires HL7 or API interface between EHR and PM, data reconciliation overhead, two vendor relationships, higher total cost.
- **Typical cost**: $150-400/provider/month for PM, plus interface development ($3,000-10,000 one-time), plus ongoing interface maintenance.

### Outsourced RCM Service

**Examples**: athenahealth RCM, R1 RCM, Ensemble Health Partners, local billing companies

- **Pros**: Expert billers and coders, handles staffing/training/turnover, typically guarantees collection rate improvements, no need to manage billing staff.
- **Cons**: Less direct control, must trust an outside party with your revenue, cost can be 5-10% of collections (which is $50,000-200,000+/year for a mid-size practice), potential communication gaps with clinical staff.
- **Best for**: Practices without experienced billing leadership, startups, practices in markets where billing talent is scarce.
- **Typical cost**: 4-10% of net collections, or $5-15 per claim, or flat per-provider fees ($800-2,500/provider/month).

### Open Source PM

**Examples**: OpenEMR (includes PM), GNU Health

- **Pros**: No licensing costs, customizable.
- **Cons**: Limited clearinghouse integrations, no built-in RCM services, requires technical staff for payer enrollment and EDI setup, limited ERA auto-posting capability.
- **Recommendation**: Not recommended for US practices unless you have dedicated development resources and deep billing expertise.

## Compliance & Regulatory Notes

- **HIPAA Transactions and Code Sets**: Claims (837P for professional), eligibility (270/271), claim status (276/277), and remittance (835) must follow HIPAA-mandated X12 EDI formats. Your PM and clearinghouse handle this, but you are ultimately responsible for compliance.
- **No Surprises Act**: As of 2022, you must provide good faith estimates to uninsured/self-pay patients. Your PM system should support generating and tracking these estimates.
- **Payer enrollment timelines**: CAQH credentialing, payer enrollment, and EDI enrollment can take 60-120 days. Start this process as early as possible when opening a new practice or onboarding new providers.
- **CPT/ICD-10 annual updates**: Your PM system must be updated annually with new codes (typically January 1 effective date). Cloud systems handle this automatically; on-premise systems may require manual updates.
- **State prompt payment laws**: Many states have laws governing how quickly payers must pay clean claims (typically 30-45 days). Your PM system should track claim aging against these benchmarks.
- **Provider NPI management**: Each provider must have an active NPI, and group practices need both Type 1 (individual) and Type 2 (organizational) NPIs properly configured in the PM system.
- **Stark Law and Anti-Kickback**: If you participate in any arrangements with referral sources that involve financial consideration, your billing practices must comply with these federal laws. This is more of a compliance program issue than a PM system issue, but your PM reporting should be able to track referral patterns.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Not verifying insurance eligibility before every visit**: Real-time eligibility checking should be integrated into your check-in workflow. This single step prevents a significant percentage of claim denials.
2. **Waiting too long to work denials**: Denials should be worked within 48-72 hours of receipt. Many practices let denials age 30+ days before touching them, losing the opportunity for timely correction and resubmission.
3. **Failing to reconcile ERA payments with expected reimbursement**: Auto-posting ERA payments is efficient, but someone must review for underpayments against contracted rates. Many practices leave 2-5% of revenue on the table by not auditing payer compliance with contracted rates.
4. **Ignoring the cost to collect**: If it costs you more to collect a $15 copay balance than the balance itself, adjust your workflow. But do not write off balances without a conscious business decision.
5. **Under-investing in coder training**: Primary care coding seems simple (99213, 99214 all day) but nuance matters. Chronic care management (99490/99491), annual wellness visits (G0438/G0439), transitional care management (99495/99496), and behavioral health integration (99484) represent significant revenue opportunities that many practices miss.
6. **Not tracking key metrics**: At minimum, track monthly: gross charges, net collections, collection rate, A/R days, denial rate by payer and reason, and provider productivity. If you are not measuring it, you cannot improve it.
7. **Choosing a PM based on cost alone**: The cheapest PM system that results in a 2% lower collection rate costs you far more than the savings. A good PM/RCM system should pay for itself through improved collections.
8. **Failing to update fee schedules annually**: Medicare and commercial rates change annually. Many practices run outdated fee schedules, either leaving money on the table or triggering payer audits for overbilling.

## Recommended Implementation Timeline

### Integrated EHR/PM (New Practice)

| Phase | Duration | Key Activities |
|-------|----------|---------------|
| Vendor selection | Concurrent with EHR (6-10 weeks) | PM evaluation as part of EHR selection |
| Payer enrollment & credentialing | 8-16 weeks (start ASAP) | CAQH, individual payer enrollment, EDI setup |
| PM configuration | 4-6 weeks (parallel with EHR build) | Fee schedules, scheduling templates, payer rules |
| Clearinghouse enrollment | 2-4 weeks | Payer-specific EDI connections |
| Staff training | 1-2 weeks | Registration, scheduling, billing workflows |
| Go-live | Same as EHR go-live | Claims submission testing with small batch first |
| Revenue cycle stabilization | 8-12 weeks | Monitor collections, refine workflows, address denials |

### Standalone PM or RCM Service Transition

| Phase | Duration | Key Activities |
|-------|----------|---------------|
| Vendor/service selection | 4-6 weeks | Evaluate PM vendors or RCM services |
| Contract negotiation | 2-4 weeks | Pricing, SLAs, data access terms |
| Configuration & enrollment | 4-8 weeks | Fee schedules, payer setup, EDI enrollment |
| Parallel processing | 4-8 weeks | Run old and new systems simultaneously |
| Cutover | 1-2 weeks | Full transition to new system |
| A/R runout from old system | 60-120 days | Continue working old A/R until resolved |

**Critical dependency**: Payer enrollment and EDI setup is the longest-lead item. For new practices, initiate CAQH profiles and payer applications 90-120 days before planned go-live. Claims cannot be submitted electronically until EDI enrollment is complete with each payer.
