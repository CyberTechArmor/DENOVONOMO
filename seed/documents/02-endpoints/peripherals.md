# Printers, Scanners & Peripherals

## What Is This?

Printers, scanners, and peripherals encompass the output and input devices that support clinical and administrative workflows in a primary care practice beyond the core workstation. This includes multifunction printers (MFPs) that print, copy, scan, and fax; dedicated label printers for specimen labels, prescription labels, and patient wristbands; high-speed document scanners for digitizing paper records, insurance cards, and incoming faxes; and specialized devices like check scanners and receipt printers.

Despite the push toward paperless healthcare, primary care practices still generate and consume significant paper. Prescriptions for non-e-prescribing scenarios (compounding pharmacies, out-of-state, certain controlled substances during system outages), patient visit summaries (AVS -- After Visit Summary), referral letters, lab requisitions, insurance forms, patient education handouts, and incoming paper correspondence all require printing and scanning capabilities. The goal is not to eliminate paper but to manage it efficiently with the right devices in the right locations.

Managed print services (MPS) have become an attractive option for practices that want predictable printing costs and outsourced maintenance. Under an MPS agreement, a provider like Xerox, Ricoh, or a local dealer places equipment in your office, handles toner replacement and maintenance, and charges a per-page fee. This eliminates capital expenditure on printers and the unpredictable cost of toner and repairs.

## Why Does a Primary Care Practice Need This?

**Printing remains essential** for several clinical and regulatory reasons. After Visit Summaries (AVS) are required by CMS for Promoting Interoperability and most practices still print them (though patient portal delivery is an alternative). Prescription printing is needed as a backup when e-prescribing is unavailable. Referral letters, prior authorization forms, and correspondence with specialists often require printed documents. Patient education materials, consent forms, and intake paperwork may still be paper-based during the transition to digital.

**Scanning is increasingly critical** as practices work to eliminate paper. Incoming paper documents -- referral letters from other practices, hospital discharge summaries, outside lab results, patient-provided records, and insurance EOBs -- must be scanned and indexed into the EHR document management system. High-speed document scanners with automatic document feeders (ADF) dramatically improve this workflow compared to using an MFP's flatbed scanner.

**Label printers** serve specific, high-value functions. Specimen labels for in-office lab work must be accurate and readable by the processing lab. Many EHR systems can print barcoded specimen labels directly to a label printer, reducing identification errors. Prescription label printers are needed for practices that dispense medications. Patient identification labels (for charts, forms, or wristbands) streamline workflows.

**Faxing** -- despite being a technology from the 1980s -- remains stubbornly entrenched in healthcare for referral management, prior authorization, and inter-provider communication. Cloud fax services (eFax, RingCentral Fax, Concord Cloud Fax) are replacing physical fax machines and integrating fax directly into the EHR.

## How to Decide If You Need It

**MFP (Multifunction Printer):** Every practice needs at least one MFP for general printing, copying, and scanning. The question is how many and where to place them.

**Dedicated document scanner:** If your practice receives more than 20-30 pages of outside paper documents per day, a dedicated high-speed scanner (Fujitsu fi-series, Canon imageFORMULA DR-series) is a major efficiency gain over using the MFP's scanner.

**Label printer:** Needed if you do in-office lab work (specimen labeling), dispense medications, or want barcode-based patient identification. Most practices with any lab function benefit from at least one label printer.

**Managed print vs. purchased equipment:** MPS makes sense for practices with 3+ MFPs that want predictable costs and zero maintenance headaches. For 1-2 devices, purchasing outright and buying toner as needed is often more economical.

## Order of Operations

1. **Audit current print/scan volumes.** Count pages printed and documents scanned per day/week. This drives device sizing and MPS cost analysis.
2. **Map printer locations.** Each clinical area (exam room pod, nursing station, front desk, lab, provider offices) needs convenient access to a printer. Walking 100 feet to pick up a printed AVS is a workflow killer.
3. **Select MFP devices.** Business-class laser MFPs from HP, Brother, Lexmark, Ricoh, or Xerox. Avoid consumer inkjet printers -- they are slow, expensive per page, and unreliable for office volumes.
4. **Select dedicated scanners** for the medical records / front office area if warranted by volume.
5. **Select label printers** for lab and clinical areas. Zebra and DYMO are the dominant brands; verify EHR compatibility before purchasing.
6. **Evaluate managed print services** if deploying 3+ MFPs. Get quotes from Xerox, Ricoh, and local dealers.
7. **Configure network printing.** Printers should be on the network (wired Ethernet preferred over Wi-Fi for reliability), with print queues managed via a print server or direct IP printing.
8. **Configure secure printing** (pull printing) if HIPAA compliance requires it -- print jobs are held on the server and released only when the user authenticates at the printer (badge tap or PIN).
9. **Implement cloud fax** to replace physical fax machines and integrate inbound faxes with the EHR.

## Options by Practice Size

**Solo / 1-2 Providers (1-2 printers):**
- One business-class MFP: HP LaserJet Pro MFP 4101fdw or Brother MFC-L5710DW ($400-600)
- One Zebra GK420d or DYMO LabelWriter 4XL for specimen labels ($200-400)
- Optional: Fujitsu ScanSnap iX1600 desktop scanner ($400-500)
- Cloud fax: eFax or RingCentral Fax ($15-30/month)
- Total: $800-1,500 plus consumables

**Small Group / 3-5 Providers (2-4 printers):**
- Two-three MFPs: HP LaserJet Enterprise MFP M430f or Lexmark MX532adwe ($600-1,200 each)
- Dedicated document scanner: Fujitsu fi-8170 ($800-1,200) at the front desk/medical records station
- Label printers at lab and check-in: Zebra ZD421 ($350-500 each)
- Consider managed print for MFPs ($0.01-0.03/page black, $0.08-0.12/page color)
- Cloud fax with EHR integration
- Total: $2,500-6,000 plus consumables or MPS contract

**Medium Group / 6-15 Providers (4-8 printers):**
- Managed print service strongly recommended: Ricoh, Xerox, or Konica Minolta dealer
- Floor-standing MFPs for high-volume areas: Ricoh IM C3010 or Xerox VersaLink C7130 ($2,000-5,000 each or included in MPS)
- Multiple document scanners for medical records and front desk
- Centralized label printing management
- Secure pull printing (PaperCut, Pharos, or vendor-native)
- Total MPS: $300-800/month all-inclusive for equipment, toner, and maintenance

## Vendor Landscape

**MFP Manufacturers:**
- **HP**: Largest market share in healthcare SMB. LaserJet Pro for small offices, LaserJet Enterprise for medium, and HP Managed Print for MPS. Strong driver support, reliable, JetIntelligence toner.
- **Brother**: Best value in desktop MFPs. MFC-L5710DW and MFC-L6800DW are workhorses. Lower toner cost per page than HP. Excellent for budget-conscious practices.
- **Lexmark**: Strong enterprise features, good security (important for healthcare). Often overlooked but competitive.
- **Ricoh**: Dominant in managed print services for healthcare. Floor-standing MFPs with advanced finishing options. Strong dealer network.
- **Xerox**: Premium MPS provider. ConnectKey platform offers good security and workflow features. Higher cost but excellent service.
- **Konica Minolta**: Strong MPS competitor to Ricoh/Xerox. bizhub series widely deployed in healthcare.

**Document Scanners:**
- **Fujitsu (PFU)**: Market leader. ScanSnap for personal/light duty, fi-series for departmental. fi-8170 ($800-1,200) is the sweet spot for medical records scanning -- 70 ppm, 80-sheet ADF, excellent paper handling.
- **Canon**: imageFORMULA DR-series. DR-C225 II ($350-450) for light duty, DR-M260 ($700-900) for medium volume.
- **Epson**: WorkForce DS-series. Competitive pricing, good for mixed document sizes. DS-790WN ($600-800) with network scanning is a strong mid-range option.

**Label Printers:**
- **Zebra Technologies**: Industry standard for healthcare labels. ZD421 (desktop, $350-500) and ZD621 (premium desktop, $600-800). Thermal transfer and direct thermal options. Best EHR compatibility.
- **DYMO**: LabelWriter series for simple address and file labels. Not recommended for specimen labels due to limited EHR integration.
- **Brother**: QL-series label printers ($80-200). Budget option with decent quality but limited healthcare-specific features.

**Cloud Fax:**
- **Concord Cloud Fax**: Purpose-built for healthcare. Direct integration with Epic, athenahealth, eClinicalWorks, and other EHRs. Inbound faxes can be automatically routed and indexed in the EHR. Premium pricing ($50-200/month) but significant workflow value.
- **RingCentral Fax**: Good general-purpose cloud fax. Integrates with RingCentral phone system. $25-35/month.
- **eFax**: Simple, affordable cloud fax. Limited EHR integration. $15-20/month.

## Compliance & Regulatory Notes

- **Secure printing** (45 CFR 164.312(a)(1)): Printed documents containing ePHI left on printer output trays are a common HIPAA violation. Implement pull printing (user authenticates at the printer before the job releases) or place printers in staff-only areas. Unclaimed print jobs should auto-delete after a set time.
- **Fax compliance**: Faxing ePHI is permitted under HIPAA but must be done carefully. Verify recipient fax numbers (misdirected faxes are a leading cause of breaches). Cloud fax with confirmation receipts and audit logging is preferable to physical fax machines.
- **Scanner security**: Network-connected scanners that scan to email or shared folders must be configured to use encrypted protocols (SMTPS for email, SMB with encryption for folder shares). Scan destinations should be access-controlled so that scanned documents are not accessible to unauthorized staff.
- **Document retention and destruction**: Paper documents that have been scanned and verified in the EHR should be destroyed per your retention policy. Use a HIPAA-compliant shredding service (Iron Mountain, Shred-it) for bulk paper destruction. Maintain certificates of destruction.
- **Printer hard drives**: Many MFPs have internal hard drives that cache printed, copied, and scanned documents. When decommissioning an MFP, the hard drive must be wiped or destroyed. Most business-class MFPs offer a "disk overwrite" security feature -- enable it.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Buying consumer inkjet printers.** Consumer inkjets are designed for occasional home use. In a medical office printing 50-200 pages per day, they are slow, expensive per page ($0.10-0.20/page vs. $0.01-0.03/page for laser), and break frequently. Business-class laser MFPs are the only appropriate choice.
2. **Placing printers too far from the point of use.** An AVS printer should be in or immediately adjacent to the exam room area. A provider who has to walk to the front desk to pick up a printout will stop printing AVS summaries, which hurts Promoting Interoperability scores.
3. **Not implementing secure printing.** A printed lab result sitting unclaimed on a shared printer tray in a hallway is a HIPAA violation. Pull printing or strategic printer placement resolves this.
4. **Ignoring per-page costs.** The purchase price of a printer is 10-20% of its lifetime cost. Toner is the real expense. Compare toner cost per page across vendors before selecting a device. High-yield toner cartridges dramatically reduce cost per page.
5. **Using the MFP as the primary document scanner.** MFP scanners are adequate for occasional scanning but too slow for digitizing stacks of medical records. A dedicated document scanner with a high-capacity ADF is 3-5x faster and handles mixed document sizes better.
6. **Not replacing physical fax machines with cloud fax.** Physical fax machines are maintenance headaches, toner consumers, and security risks (inbound faxes sitting in a tray). Cloud fax delivers inbound faxes as PDF files directly to the EHR or a secure inbox.
7. **Forgetting to wipe MFP hard drives.** When you return a leased MFP or dispose of a purchased one, the internal hard drive may contain cached copies of every document printed, copied, or scanned. This is a data breach if the drive is not wiped.

## Recommended Implementation Timeline

**Weeks 1-2: Assessment**
- Audit current print volumes by location (use existing printer counters or manual tracking)
- Map printer locations needed based on clinical and administrative workflow
- Evaluate managed print vs. purchase decision
- Assess document scanning volume and need for dedicated scanners

**Weeks 3-4: Procurement**
- Select and order MFPs, document scanners, and label printers
- If MPS: sign agreement, schedule dealer installation
- Select and sign up for cloud fax service
- Order toner stock and label supplies

**Weeks 5-6: Installation and Configuration**
- Install devices on the network (wired Ethernet connection for each)
- Configure print queues on the server or via direct IP printing
- Configure scan-to-email and scan-to-folder destinations with encryption
- Set up label printers with EHR label templates (specimen labels, patient labels)
- Configure cloud fax integration with EHR

**Week 7: Security and Testing**
- Enable secure printing / pull printing if implemented
- Enable disk overwrite security on MFPs with hard drives
- Test every printer from every workstation
- Test document scanner workflow end-to-end (scan, index, verify in EHR)
- Test label printing from EHR

**Week 8: Training**
- Train clinical staff on label printing workflow
- Train medical records staff on document scanning and indexing
- Train all staff on secure printing procedures
- Train staff on cloud fax -- sending, receiving, and routing inbound faxes

**Ongoing:**
- Monthly toner inventory check (or automatic replenishment via MPS)
- Quarterly review of print volumes and costs
- Annual MPS contract review and renegotiation
- Replace desktop MFPs at year 4-5; floor-standing MFPs typically last 5-7 years under MPS
